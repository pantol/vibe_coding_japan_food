Chapter 6

Japan's Best-Kept Culinary Secret

Oita Prefecture is one of Japan's best-kept culinary secrets. Tucked into Kyushu's northeastern corner, its dramatic terrain — the Kuju mountains, volcanic hot springs, the Bungo Channel coast — produces everything from shiitake mushrooms and kabosu citrus to world-class mackerel.

Toriten (chicken tempura) is the signature dish, originating in a Beppu restaurant in the early 1920s. Unlike standard tempura, toriten uses marinated chicken pieces coated in a light batter and fried until golden, then dipped in a tangy ponzu or kabosu-vinegar sauce that cuts through the richness.

The prefecture's volcanic geography is not just scenery — it shapes the cuisine itself. In Beppu, locals have cooked food over natural hot spring vents for centuries, a technique called jigoku-mushi (hell steaming). Meanwhile, the fast-moving currents of the Bungo Channel produce some of Japan's most prized fish, while highland pastures yield beef that rivals Kobe at a fraction of the price.

Toriten

とり天

Chicken coated in light tempura batter and fried until golden — Oita's flagship street dish. Originating in a Beppu restaurant in the early 1920s, toriten is dipped in a tangy kabosu-vinegar sauce.

Signature Dish ¥
Origin

Beppu, early 1920s. Born in a local restaurant as a twist on traditional tempura.

Flavor

Light, crispy batter with juicy marinated chicken. Dipped in tangy kabosu-vinegar sauce that cuts through the richness.

Setting

Casual restaurants and street food stalls throughout Beppu and Oita city.

Where

Toyotsune (birthplace), Waon

Jigoku-Mushi

地獄蒸し

Hell steaming: cooking food over volcanic hot spring vents. Eggs, seafood, and vegetables are placed in bamboo baskets and steamed by natural geothermal heat. An ancient technique documented over 250 years ago.

Ancient Technique ¥
Origin

Kannawa district, Beppu. Documented over 250 years ago as a cooking method using natural volcanic steam.

Flavor

Pure, mineral-infused steam preserves natural flavors. Eggs, seafood, and vegetables emerge tender with subtle volcanic terroir.

Setting

Public steam cooking workshops where you buy ingredients and steam them yourself over volcanic vents.

Where

Jigoku Mushi Kobo Kannawa

Seki-Aji / Seki-Saba

関あじ・関さば

Prized horse mackerel and mackerel from the fast-moving currents of the Bungo Channel. Among Japan's most valued fish, their firm flesh and rich flavor command premium prices nationwide.

Premium Seafood ¥¥
Origin

Saganoseki fishing port, where the Bungo Channel's powerful currents produce exceptionally firm, flavorful fish.

Flavor

Firm, dense flesh with clean, rich flavor. Best enjoyed as sashimi to appreciate the texture and sweetness.

Setting

Seafood restaurants in Oita city and Saganoseki. Peak freshness near the port.

Where

Seafood Izutsu

Bungo-Gyu

Ryukyu

りゅうきゅう

Marinated raw fish served over rice. Tuna or other fresh fish is aged in sake lees, soy, and sesame, producing intense umami and a distinctive sake aroma.

Local Classic ¥
Origin

Oita's fishing communities. A preservation technique that evolved into a beloved local delicacy.

Flavor

Intense umami with sake aroma. Rich sesame and soy marinade coats silky raw fish over warm rice.

Setting

Izakayas and set-meal restaurants. Often served as part of a teishoku lunch.

Where

Waon, local izakayas throughout Oita city

Dangojiru

だんご汁

Soul-warming miso soup with flattened wheat dumplings and root vegetables. Born in rustic farming communities as comfort food, it remains a beloved staple of Oita home cooking.

Comfort Food ¥
Origin

Rural farming communities of Oita. A hearty peasant dish that sustained mountain villagers through cold winters.

Flavor

Rich, savory miso broth with chewy flattened wheat dumplings and earthy root vegetables. Deeply warming.

Setting

Home kitchens and casual local restaurants. Best on a cold day after hiking the Kuju mountains.

Where

Amamichaya, local shokudo (family diners) across the prefecture

Street Food & Markets

Where the Prefecture Eats

Yufuin Morning Markets

Set against the stunning backdrop of Mount Yufu, Yufuin's morning markets offer fresh local produce, handmade pickles, shiitake mushrooms, and kabosu citrus products. A peaceful start to the day in one of Japan's most charming onsen towns.

B-Con Plaza Farmers' Market

A weekend gathering of local farmers and producers in Beppu. Bungo beef skewers sizzle alongside stalls of kabosu, fresh vegetables, and homemade confections. A window into Oita's agricultural soul.

Beppu Onsen Street Food

Wander the steam-filled streets of Beppu's onsen districts for hell-steamed eggs, toriten from hole-in-the-wall shops, and soft-serve ice cream flavored with kabosu. The volcanic steam rising from every corner makes eating here an otherworldly experience.

Local Drinks

What to Drink

🍶

Barley Shochu

Oita is one of Japan's great shochu regions. Kitsuki town produces exceptional barley shochu — smooth, earthy, and best enjoyed on the rocks or with warm water.

🍶

Local Sake

Small breweries across the prefecture produce clean, crisp sake using mountain spring water filtered through volcanic rock. Pairs beautifully with seki-aji sashimi.

🍷

Fruit Liqueurs

Oita's mild climate produces excellent fruits. Local ume (plum) wines and even banana wines offer a sweet, aromatic alternative to spirits.

🍵

Yuzu Sake

Warm citrus-infused wine made with locally grown yuzu. A soothing drink especially welcome after a long soak in an onsen on a cold evening.

Dining Etiquette

Rules of the Table

Ryokan Banquets

Respect the multi-course progression — light dishes come first, rice is served last. Each course is timed to the kitchen's rhythm. Don't rush or rearrange the order.

Kyushu Warmth

Kyushu people are warm and chatty. Don't be surprised if locals strike up conversation at izakayas. Engage openly — it's part of the dining culture here.

General Rules

No smoking indoors unless in a designated area. Say "gochisosama" (thank you for the meal) when finished and return bowls to their original positions.

Onsen Dining

If dining at a ryokan after bathing, arrive in your yukata (robe). It's expected and comfortable. Take your time — onsen dining is meant to be savored slowly.
