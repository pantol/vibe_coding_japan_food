Chapter 1

The Capital of Endless Appetite

Tokyo holds 194 Michelin-starred restaurants with a combined 251 stars — more than any other city on earth, a title it has held for 19 consecutive years since the guide’s first non-Western edition in 2007. Yet the city’s culinary DNA is rooted not in haute cuisine but in street food, and some of its greatest meals are served on plastic trays in rooms with six seats and no sign outside.

During the Edo period (1603–1868), when the city was home to over a million people, laborers and samurai ate at yatai stalls selling vinegared-rice sushi, crispy tempura, and slippery soba noodles. Edomae sushi — fish from “in front of Edo” (Tokyo Bay), cured or pickled for preservation, pressed onto rice seasoned with red vinegar — was born at these stalls in the 1820s. The post-war period transformed Tokyo again: American wheat supplies catalyzed ramen’s explosive rise, while Western yoshoku cuisine — tonkatsu, curry rice, omurice — became mainstream comfort food. The 1964 Olympics internationalized the dining scene.

The result is a city where a ¥900 bowl of tsukemen (dipping noodles invented in the 1950s by Kazuo Yamagishi at Taishoken in Ikebukuro) and a ¥30,000 dinner at Narisawa both represent Tokyo at its finest. The shokunin (craftsman) ethos means that a ramen shop may serve only one style of broth, perfected over decades. A tempura counter seats eight people and serves nothing else. This single-minded devotion to craft is what makes Tokyo’s food scene so extraordinary.

Edomae Sushi

江戸前寿司

Born in the 1820s as a quick street snack, nigiri-zushi was originally preserved with vinegar and salt. Today's omakase counters honor that legacy while working with pristine fish from Toyosu Market.

Signature Dish ¥¥¥¥
Origin

Edo period, 1820s street carts in Ryogoku

Flavor

Vinegared rice with raw seafood, seasoned with nikiri soy and wasabi. Pure, clean, focused.

Setting

Counter seating at sushi-ya. Eat with hands or chopsticks. Best at Ginza or Tsukiji area.

Where

Sukiyabashi Jiro, Sushi Saito, Hakkoku, Sushi Dai (budget)

Ramen (Shoyu)

醤油ラーメン

Tokyo's signature shoyu ramen features a clear, dark, deeply savory soy-based broth with curly medium noodles, chashu pork, menma bamboo shoots, nori, and a soft egg.

Comfort Food ¥
Origin

Chinese noodle houses of the early 1900s, adapted with Japanese ingredients

Flavor

Dark, savory soy-based broth with pork fat richness. Aromatic and deeply umami.

Setting

Narrow ramen-ya shops, often with ticket machines. Slurp loudly. Eat quickly.

Where

Iruca Tokyo, Sobahouse Konjiki Hototogisu, Rokurinsha (tsukemen), Kiraku

Tempura

天ぷら

Edo-period tempura was street food fried in sesame oil over charcoal. The finest tempura-ya today maintain that sesame oil tradition, producing impossibly light, greaseless results.

Edo Classic ¥¥¥
Origin

Edo-period food stalls on Tokyo Bay. One of the three Edo delicacies.

Flavor

Light, airy batter fried in sesame oil. Preserves the pure taste of seafood and vegetables.

Setting

Counter seating at specialty tempura restaurants. Served piece by piece.

Where

Tempura Kondo (2 Michelin stars, Ginza)

Tonkatsu

とんかつ

Breaded and deep-fried pork cutlet adapted from European cuisine during the Meiji era, elevated to an art form. The city debates rosu (fatty loin) versus hire (lean tenderloin).

Meiji Era ¥¥
Origin

Adapted from European cuisine during the Meiji era (1868–1912)

Flavor

Crispy panko exterior, juicy pork interior. Served with shredded cabbage and tonkatsu sauce.

Setting

Dedicated tonkatsu shops. Choose rosu or hire, then add rice, miso soup, and cabbage.

Where

Tonkatsu Tonta (low-temperature specialists, Nerima-ku)

Yakitori

焼き鳥

Grilled chicken skewers reach their apotheosis in Tokyo's smoky, intimate yakitori-ya beneath the train tracks. Each cut requires different heat and timing on binchotan charcoal.

Izakaya Classic ¥
Origin

Post-war Tokyo izakaya culture. Elevated to an art form at specialist counters.

Flavor

Charcoal-grilled chicken (thigh, skin, heart, cartilage, tail) with tare sauce or salt.

Setting

Tiny counters under railway tracks, or upscale yakitori-ya in Ginza.

Where

Bird Land Ginza (Michelin), Yurakucho yakitori alley (budget)

Soba

蕎麦

Buckwheat noodles carry almost philosophical weight in Tokyo. The finest versions use freshly ground, stone-milled buckwheat with minimal wheat flour to bind it.

Edo Tradition ¥¥
Origin

Edo period soba shops served as neighborhood gathering places since mid-1600s

Flavor

Earthy, nutty buckwheat with firm texture. Served cold (zaru) or hot in broth. Slurp to enhance aroma.

Setting

Small soba counters. Eat promptly. The craft is in the flour milling and kneading.

Where

Specialty soba shops in Kanda, Nihonbashi areas

Monjayaki

もんじゃ焼き

Tokyo's messy, beloved answer to Osaka's okonomiyaki. Originating in Tsukishima as a children's snack, it uses a much thinner batter that stays gooey on the teppan griddle.

Tokyo Original ¥
Origin

Tsukishima neighborhood, early 20th century. Originally a children's after-school snack.

Flavor

Savory, chewy, gooey batter with seafood, cabbage, and sauce. Scraped off the grill with metal spatulas.

Setting

Communal hotplate tables in Tsukishima (Tokyo's monja district).

Where

Tsukishima Monja Street (dozens of shops)

Street Food & Markets

Where the City Eats

Tsukiji Outer Market

Tokyo's most vibrant food destination even after the wholesale market's relocation to Toyosu. Narrow lanes packed with vendors selling tamagoyaki, fresh sashimi on sticks, grilled scallops, and exceptional Japanese knives. Go by 7 a.m. for the best experience.

Ameyoko Market (Ueno)

Tokyo's scrappiest food street — a chaotic, postwar black-market corridor now selling everything from fresh seafood and dried fruits to chocolate and kebabs. Not refined, but real. Energy peaks in late December before New Year.

Omoide Yokocho

"Memory Lane" near Shinjuku Station. Tiny alleyway bars and yakitori stalls in a nostalgic post-war setting. Smoke, cold beer, and charcoal-grilled skewers. Best visited after 6 p.m. on weekdays.

Local Drinks

What to Drink

🍶

Sake

Tokyo draws the best bottles from Niigata, Yamagata, and Akita. Standing sake bars (tachinomi) around Shinbashi and Yurakucho are ideal classrooms.

🍺

Craft Beer

Booming craft beer scene with breweries like Baird Beer. Also a thriving whiskey highball culture at standing bars.

🍵

Matcha

Matcha lattes in specialty cafes. Also ramune soda with summer foods and hojicha (roasted green tea) in winter.

🥃

Whiskey Highball

Japanese whiskey served as highball (with soda) is the city's preferred refreshment at izakayas. Suntory and Nikka lead.

Dining Etiquette

Rules of the Table

At the Sushi Counter

Eat nigiri with your hands if you prefer — it's traditional. Never dip rice-side down if the chef has already applied nikiri soy. Trust the chef's sequence.

Noodle Etiquette

Slurping noodles is not only acceptable but encouraged — it cools the noodles and enhances aroma. Eat promptly; don't let ramen sit.

General Rules

No tipping. Say "itadakimasu" before eating and "gochisosama" after. Never stick chopsticks upright in rice. Don't pass food chopstick-to-chopstick.

Izakaya Culture

Order a drink when you sit down. Pour for others before yourself. Share dishes communally. Eating promptly and sharing seating at communal bars is normal.
