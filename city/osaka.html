Chapter 2

The Nation's Kitchen

Osaka’s relationship with food is summed up in a single word: kuidaore — “eat until you drop,” or more precisely, “ruin yourself through extravagance in food.” While Kyoto’s saying is “kidaore” (spend on clothing) and Tokyo’s is “keidaore” (spend on fashion), Osaka’s identity centers on eating. During the Edo period, Osaka functioned as Japan’s central rice-trade hub — the “Nation’s Kitchen” (tenka no daidokoro) — with ingredients arriving via the Kitamaebune sea routes from every corner of Japan.

The city’s powerful merchant class, unlike the samurai of Edo or the aristocrats of Kyoto, demanded food that was bold, generous, and accessible. This produced a democratic food culture built on konamon (flour-based dishes): takoyaki, okonomiyaki, negiyaki. The merchant mentality also shaped Osaka’s distinctive dashi — the monopolization of makombu kelp from Hokkaido created a lighter, kombu-forward broth that is the foundation of kitsune udon and the entire Kansai flavor profile, contrasting sharply with Tokyo’s darker, bonito-heavy style.

Osaka consumes roughly 60% of Japan’s fugu supply, has over 700 takoyaki shops, and the city’s innovations include conveyor-belt sushi, instant ramen, and commercial takoyaki. This is the democratic table — food for everyone, meant to be shared loudly and devoured without pretense.

Takoyaki

たこ焼き

Crispy-skinned, molten-centered octopus balls. Invented in the 1930s by Endo Tomekichi. Topped with takoyaki sauce, mayo, and dancing bonito flakes. Osaka's undisputed street food icon.

Street Food Icon ¥
Origin

Invented 1935 by street vendor Endo Tomekichi in Osaka

Flavor

Crispy shell, molten dough center with tender octopus. Rich sauce, creamy mayo, umami bonito flakes.

Setting

Street stalls and standing counters. Eaten with toothpicks, piping hot. Careful of the molten center.

Where

Abeno Takoyaki Yamachan, Takoyaki Wanaka

Okonomiyaki

お好み焼き

Savory pancake — "grill what you like." Cabbage and flour batter loaded with meat or seafood. Slathered with thick fruity sauce, mayo, and katsuobushi. DIY at teppan tables.

Osaka Soul Food ¥¥
Origin

Evolved from pre-war "issen yoshoku" one-coin Western food. Osaka-style mixes all ingredients together.

Flavor

Savory cabbage-rich pancake with thick fruity sauce, creamy mayo, and dancing katsuobushi flakes.

Setting

Cook-it-yourself at teppan griddle tables, or let the chef handle it. Social, lively atmosphere.

Where

Ajinoya, Mizuno

Kushikatsu

串カツ

Deep-fried skewers born in 1920s Shinsekai as working-class food. Fine panko, light fry, endless variety. Cardinal rule: absolutely no double dipping in the communal sauce.

Shinsekai Classic ¥
Origin

1920s Shinsekai district, created as affordable food for laborers and workers

Flavor

Lightly panko-breaded skewers of meat, seafood, and vegetables. Crispy, not greasy. Dipped in tangy Worcestershire-based sauce.

Setting

Standing counters in Shinsekai. Communal sauce pot — one dip only. Use cabbage leaf to scoop extra sauce.

Where

Kushikatsu Daruma, Yaekatsu

Kitsune Udon

きつねうどん

Fat chewy wheat noodles in pale konbu-rich dashi, topped with sweet simmered aburaage (fried tofu). Originated at Matsubaaya in the 1890s. Simple, soul-warming, quintessentially Osaka.

Comfort Classic ¥
Origin

Created at Matsubaaya in the 1890s, one of Osaka's oldest udon traditions

Flavor

Delicate konbu-rich dashi broth, chewy thick udon noodles, sweet and savory simmered aburaage tofu.

Setting

Traditional udon shops. Slurp freely. The broth is lighter than Tokyo's soy-heavy style.

Where

Nishiya

Hamo

鱧

Pike conger, peaking during Tenjin Matsuri in July. A delicate white fish requiring the bone-cut technique (honegiri) to slice through hundreds of tiny bones while keeping the flesh intact.

Seasonal Delicacy ¥¥¥
Origin

Summer tradition tied to Tenjin Matsuri festival in July. Prized in Kansai cuisine for centuries.

Flavor

Delicate, refined white fish. Light and clean. Often served as tempura, shabu-shabu, or with ume (plum) sauce.

Setting

Upscale kaiseki restaurants and traditional Japanese dining rooms during summer months.

Where

Seasonal — available at fine dining establishments across Osaka in summer

Fugu

ふぐ

Pufferfish, the winter specialty that requires licensed chefs to prepare. Osaka is Japan's biggest fugu market. Served as translucent sashimi (tessa), hot pot (tecchiri), or deep-fried (karaage).

Winter Specialty ¥¥¥
Origin

Osaka is the center of Japan's fugu culture, consuming more pufferfish than any other city

Flavor

Subtle, clean, delicate. Tessa (sashimi) is paper-thin. Tecchiri (hot pot) extracts deep umami from bones and skin.

Setting

Specialist fugu restaurants, October through March. Licensed chefs only. A winter ritual.

Where

Seasonal — specialist fugu restaurants throughout Osaka, particularly around Dotonbori

Street Food & Markets

Where the City Eats

Dotonbori

Osaka's neon-lit food circus along the canal. Giant mechanical crabs, glowing gyoza signs, and the iconic Glico Running Man set the scene for an endless parade of takoyaki, okonomiyaki, and kushikatsu stalls. Best experienced at dusk when the lights come alive.

Kuromon Ichiba Market

Known as "Osaka's Kitchen," this covered market has served the city for over a century. Fresh seafood, takoyaki, grilled skewers, seasonal fruit, and fugu. The stalls are best visited early morning before the tourist crowds arrive.

Shinsekai

A retro district built around the Tsutenkaku Tower, Shinsekai is the spiritual home of kushikatsu. Neon signs, standing bars, and deep-fried everything. Working-class charm and unapologetic indulgence — pure Osaka energy.

Local Drinks

What to Drink

🍺

Asahi Beer

Brewed in Suita since 1889, Asahi is Osaka's hometown beer. The crisp, dry Super Dry was born here and pairs perfectly with kushikatsu and takoyaki.

🍶

Sake from Fushimi

Nearby Fushimi (Kyoto) supplies Osaka with exceptional sake. Soft water produces a mellow, slightly sweet style that complements Osaka's bold flavors.

🍻

Minoh Beer

Award-winning craft brewery from the Minoh hills north of Osaka. Their stout and pale ale have won international acclaim. Osaka's craft beer pioneer.

🥃

Tachinomi (Standing Bars)

Standing bars in Tenma and Kyobashi are Osaka's after-work ritual. Cheap beer, highballs, and small plates. Shoulder-to-shoulder with salarymen. The real Osaka.

Sightseeing

Attractions

Beyond the food, Osaka and its surroundings offer iconic landmarks, retro neighborhoods, and nature escapes.

Osaka Castle

大阪城

Built in 1583 by Toyotomi Hideyoshi as the largest fortress of its time, Osaka Castle played a key role in the unification of Japan. Destroyed and rebuilt multiple times — the current 1931 concrete reconstruction houses a museum and elevator inside.

Historic Landmark Osaka-jo
History

Originally built 1583 by Toyotomi Hideyoshi as the largest fortress of its era. Destroyed and rebuilt multiple times; current version dates to 1931.

Highlights

Panoramic city view from the 8th floor observation deck. Nishinomaru Garden is one of the best hanami spots with approximately 600 cherry trees.

Tip

Buy tickets online on Klook to skip the queue. The museum inside covers the history of Toyotomi Hideyoshi and the castle.

Dotonbori & Shinsaibashi

道頓堀

The heart of entertainment Osaka — a canal district surrounded by neons and giant signs, including the famous Glico Running Man (since 1935). Osaka is the “nation’s kitchen” (tenka no daidokoro), and this is its street food epicenter.

Entertainment District Namba
Highlights

Iconic neon-lit canal with giant signs, the Glico Running Man billboard (since 1935), and an endless parade of takoyaki, okonomiyaki, and kushikatsu stalls.

Shopping

Shinsaibashi-Suji is one of the oldest and longest covered shopping arcades in Japan, stretching 600 meters with hundreds of shops.

Food

Street food epicenter for takoyaki, okonomiyaki, and kushikatsu. Best experienced at dusk when the lights come alive.

Shinsekai

新世界

A retro district built in 1912 as a futuristic city modeled on New York (north) and Paris (south). Home to Tsutenkaku Tower (103m) with the Billiken lucky god statue at its base — touching his feet is said to bring luck.

Retro District Kushikatsu Mecca
History

Built 1912 as a futuristic city modeled on New York (northern half) and Paris (southern half). The retro atmosphere today is pure Osaka nostalgia.

Highlights

Tsutenkaku Tower (103m) with the Billiken lucky god statue — rub his feet for good luck. The kushikatsu capital of Osaka.

Tip

Golden rule: NEVER double-dip your kushikatsu in the shared sauce! Use cabbage leaves to scoop extra sauce instead.

Umeda Sky Building

梅田スカイビル

A futuristic 173-meter skyscraper from 1993 designed by Hiroshi Hara. Two towers connected by a “floating” rooftop garden observatory (Kuchu Teien). Named one of the world’s 20 best buildings by the Times.

Architecture Panoramic Views
Architecture

Designed by Hiroshi Hara in 1993. Two towers connected by the “floating” Kuchu Teien (Garden in the Sky) rooftop observatory.

Highlights

360-degree panorama from the 40th floor. Named one of the world’s 20 best buildings by the Times. In the evening, the floor glows with phosphorescent stars.

Location

Umeda district, northern Osaka. The building is a striking landmark visible from across the city skyline.

Akame 48 Waterfalls

赤目四十八滝

A valley in Mie Prefecture, part of Muro-Akame-Aoyama Quasi-National Park. A 4km trail along the Takigawa River passes a series of waterfalls. Historic ninja training ground for the Iga-ryu school (15th–18th century).

Nature & History Day Trip
Waterfalls

Five main waterfalls: Fudo (15m), Senju (15m), Nunobiki, Ninai, and Biwa. The name Akame (“red eyes”) comes from a legend of a monk meeting a fire deity on a red-eyed bull.

Wildlife

Japanese giant salamanders live in the rivers (up to 1.8m long, protected species). A museum at the entrance is included in the ¥1,000 ticket.

Tip

Trail takes 3–4 hours round trip. Good footwear is essential. Historically a ninja training ground for the Iga-ryu school (15th–18th century).

Minoo Park

箕面公園

A park on Osaka’s northern outskirts featuring the 33-meter Minoo Falls. A gentle 2.8km trail from the station passes the historic Ryuanji temple and Togetsukyo bridge. Local specialty: momiji tempura — deep-fried maple leaves.

Nature Day Trip
Trail

2.8km gentle walk from Minoo Station to the waterfall. Pass the historic Ryuanji temple and Togetsukyo bridge along the way. About 3 hours round trip.

Highlights

33-meter Minoo Falls, spectacular autumn colors, and home to Japanese macaques. Autumn is the most popular season to visit.

Food

Try the local specialty: momiji tempura — deep-fried maple leaves. Yes, really! A unique and surprisingly delicious snack.

Dining Etiquette

Rules of the Table

Kuidaore Spirit

Eat boldly and without hesitation. Osaka's food culture rewards enthusiasm. Try everything, order generously, and don't hold back — restraint is not an Osaka virtue.

No Double Dipping

At kushikatsu counters, the sauce is communal. Dip your skewer once only. Use the cabbage leaves provided to scoop extra sauce if needed. This rule is sacred.

General Rules

No tipping. Say "gochisosama" when finished. Never stick chopsticks upright in rice. Share tables freely in busy areas — Osakans are communal eaters.

Train Etiquette

No eating on trains or while walking in stations. Finish your takoyaki at the stall before moving on. Eating is for standing still or sitting down, not commuting.
