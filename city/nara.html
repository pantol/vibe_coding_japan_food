Nara

奈良

Japan’s First Permanent Capital

Overview Attractions
Ancient Capital

Where Japan’s Imperial History Began

Nara served as Japan’s first permanent capital from 710 to 784, during the Nara Period — an era that saw the flourishing of Buddhism, the compilation of Japan’s earliest written histories, and the establishment of cultural traditions that would shape the nation for over a millennium. Known then as Heijō-kyō, the city was modeled after the Chinese Tang Dynasty capital of Chang’an and became the political and cultural heart of early Japan.

Today, Nara is best known for its expansive deer park, where over 1,200 sacred sika deer roam freely among visitors, ancient temples, and wooded paths. According to Shinto legend, the god Takemikazuchi arrived in Nara riding atop a white deer in 768 AD, and since then the deer have been revered as divine messengers. They are officially designated as National Natural Monuments.

The city’s major temples and shrines — Todai-ji with its colossal bronze Buddha, the lantern-lined paths of Kasuga Taisha, and the towering pagoda of Kofuku-ji — are UNESCO World Heritage Sites that draw millions of visitors each year. Nara is compact and walkable, making it an ideal day trip from Kyoto or Osaka, though those who stay overnight are rewarded with quiet mornings among the deer before the crowds arrive.

Nara Park (Nara-koen)

奈良公園

Over 1,200 sacred sika deer roam freely across this vast parkland, recognized as Divine Messengers of the Kasuga Shrine. According to legend, the god Takemikazuchi arrived on a white deer in 768 AD. The deer are designated as Natural Monuments and will bow for crackers — though they can be pushy!

Park & Wildlife Free
Origin

Sacred deer have inhabited Nara since 768 AD, when the deity Takemikazuchi was said to have arrived on a white deer.

Highlights

Buy shika senbei (deer crackers, ~¥200) to feed the deer. They bow before receiving treats. Best visited early morning when crowds are thin and deer are calm.

Tips

Open 24/7, free admission. Keep food hidden until ready to feed — deer will crowd you. Secure loose maps and papers. Be cautious during rutting season (autumn).

Where

Central Nara, a 5-minute walk east of Kintetsu Nara Station

Todai-ji

東大寺

Home to the Daibutsu — the world’s largest bronze Buddha statue. Standing 15 meters tall, weighing approximately 500 tons, and originally cast in 752 AD, this awe-inspiring figure sits within one of the largest wooden structures on earth.

Buddhist Temple ¥600
Origin

Founded in 752 by Emperor Shōmu as the head temple of all provincial Buddhist temples in Japan.

Highlights

The Great Buddha Hall (Daibutsuden) is one of the world’s largest wooden buildings, despite being only two-thirds its original size. Try squeezing through the pillar hole — said to grant enlightenment.

Tips

Hours: 7:30–17:30 (varies seasonally; opens 8:00 Nov–Feb). Arrive early to avoid tour groups. The approach through Nandaimon gate with its guardian statues is spectacular.

Where

406-1 Zoshicho, Nara — within Nara Park, 20 minutes on foot from Kintetsu Nara Station

Kasuga Taisha

春日大社

One of Japan’s most celebrated Shinto shrines, famous for its thousands of stone and bronze lanterns donated by worshippers over the centuries. The forested approach is one of Nara’s most atmospheric walks, with moss-covered lanterns lining the path through ancient cedar trees.

Shinto Shrine Free (inner: ¥500)
Origin

Established in 768 AD as the tutelary shrine of the powerful Fujiwara clan. One of the three great Kasuga festivals has been held here for over 1,000 years.

Highlights

Over 3,000 stone and bronze lanterns illuminate the shrine grounds. All lanterns are lit twice a year during the Mantoro (Lantern) Festivals in early February and mid-August.

Tips

The grounds are free to explore; the inner sanctuary costs ¥500. The Botanical Garden (¥500) features wisteria in late April–May. Visit at dusk for magical light.

Where

160 Kasuganocho, Nara — at the eastern edge of Nara Park, a 30-minute walk from Kintetsu Nara Station

Kofuku-ji

興福寺

This historic Buddhist temple complex features a striking five-story pagoda — the second tallest in Japan at 50 meters — and a beautifully restored Central Golden Hall. The pagoda is one of Nara’s most iconic silhouettes, especially when reflected in Sarusawa Pond at dusk.

Buddhist Temple ¥700
Origin

Founded in 669 by the Fujiwara clan, relocated to Nara in 710 when the capital was established. Once one of the most powerful temples in Japan with over 150 buildings.

Highlights

The National Treasure Museum houses extraordinary Buddhist sculptures, including the famous three-headed, six-armed Ashura statue. The Central Golden Hall was reconstructed in 2018.

Tips

The pagoda and grounds are free to view from outside. Museum and hall admission is ¥700 (combined). Located right next to Sarusawa Pond — lovely for photos at sunset.

Where

48 Noboriojicho, Nara — immediately adjacent to Kintetsu Nara Station, the first major site most visitors encounter

Visitor Tip

Nara Park is open 24/7 and free to enter. Todai-ji hours: 7:30–17:30 (seasonal). Leave backpacks and large bags in station coin lockers to keep your hands free — the deer will investigate anything that rustles or smells like food.
