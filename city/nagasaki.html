Where East Met West

Japan’s Gateway to the World

Nagasaki served as Japan’s sole gateway to the Western world during more than 200 years of national isolation under the sakoku policy. While the rest of the country was sealed off from foreign contact, this port city on Kyushu’s western coast quietly absorbed the ideas, technologies, and cultures of distant lands — creating a unique blend of Japanese, Chinese, Dutch, and Portuguese influences found nowhere else in Japan.

The city’s international heritage runs deep. Portuguese missionaries arrived in the 16th century, establishing Nagasaki as the center of Christianity in Japan. Chinese merchants built temples and trading communities that still thrive today. Dutch traders, confined to the tiny artificial island of Dejima, became the country’s only sanctioned link to European science, medicine, and technology for over two centuries.

Nagasaki also bears the weight of being the site of the second atomic bombing on August 9, 1945. Like Hiroshima, the city has rebuilt itself as a powerful advocate for peace and nuclear disarmament. Today, Nagasaki is a city of layered histories — where ancient temples sit alongside Western-style churches, where cobblestone slopes recall a Dutch enclave, and where the memory of devastation fuels an enduring commitment to a peaceful world.

Attractions

Nagasaki’s landmarks reveal centuries of cross-cultural exchange and a profound commitment to peace.

Peace Park

平和公園

On August 9, 1945, three days after Hiroshima, the plutonium bomb “Fat Man” was dropped on Nagasaki, killing approximately 40,000 people instantly — a figure that rose to around 70,000 by the end of the year. The epicenter of the blast was directly over the Urakami district, home to the city’s largest Christian community and the Urakami Cathedral.

Peace Memorial Urakami District
Sculpture

The main Peace Statue, a 10-meter bronze figure by sculptor Kitamura Seibo, is the park’s centerpiece. The right hand points to the sky, symbolizing the threat of nuclear weapons, while the left hand extends horizontally, representing the hope for peace. The closed eyes offer a prayer for the victims.

Nearby

The Nagasaki Atomic Bomb Museum chronicles the bombing and its aftermath through artifacts, photographs, and survivor testimonies. The Peace Fountain at the park’s entrance is dedicated to the countless victims who died of thirst, desperately seeking water in the firestorm that followed the blast.

Hours

Museum open 8:30–17:30. The park itself is free to enter and accessible at all hours. Reserve at least 1–2 hours for the museum.

Advice

As with Hiroshima, prepare yourself emotionally. The museum contains graphic material and deeply personal accounts. The park and its monuments provide a space for quiet reflection afterwards.

Glover Garden & Oura Cathedral

グラバー園 & 大浦天主堂

An open-air museum showcasing 19th-century Western residences, including the house of Thomas Glover — a Scottish merchant who played a pivotal role in Japan’s modernization by supplying arms and ships to the rebels who ultimately overthrew the Tokugawa shogunate. His love story with a Japanese woman named Tsuru reportedly inspired Puccini’s opera “Madame Butterfly.”

UNESCO Related National Treasure
Glover Garden

Perched on a hillside overlooking Nagasaki Harbor, the garden contains several relocated and restored Western-style homes from the Meiji era. The Glover House (1863) is the oldest surviving Western-style building in Japan. The grounds offer panoramic views of the port and city below.

Oura Cathedral

Built in 1864 by French missionaries, Oura Cathedral is the oldest surviving church in Japan and a designated National Treasure. It is part of the UNESCO World Heritage “Hidden Christian Sites in the Nagasaki Region,” honoring the communities that practiced Christianity in secret for over 200 years during the ban on the faith.

Duration

Allow approximately 1 hour to explore Glover Garden and Oura Cathedral together. The two sites are adjacent and can be visited in a single trip.

Setting

The hillside location provides beautiful views, especially in the late afternoon light. Escalators help with the steep climb. A lovely spot to appreciate Nagasaki’s unique East-meets-West atmosphere.

Dejima

出島

A fan-shaped artificial island built in 1636, Dejima was Japan’s only window to the Western world for more than 200 years of national isolation (sakoku, 1641–1854). The Dutch East India Company operated its trading post here, and the Dutch traders were essentially prisoners — confined to the tiny island and unable to leave without special permission from Japanese authorities.

Historic Trading Post Sakoku Era
Significance

Through this small island, Western science, medicine, and technology entered Japan during the centuries of isolation. This exchange, known as rangaku (“Dutch learning”), had a profound influence on Japanese intellectual life, introducing everything from anatomy and astronomy to painting techniques and botanical knowledge.

Today

The island has been extensively reconstructed to show what life was like for the Dutch merchants and their Japanese hosts. Restored buildings include warehouses, residences, and a chief factor’s quarters, furnished with period artifacts and interactive exhibitions.

Duration

Allow approximately 1 hour to explore the reconstructed buildings and exhibitions. Audio guides are available in multiple languages.

Context

Understanding Dejima is key to understanding how Japan maintained contact with the outside world during its long period of seclusion — and how that limited contact shaped the country’s eventual rapid modernization in the Meiji era.

Sofuku-ji, Megane Bridge, Dutch Slope

崇福寺・眼鏡橋・オランダ坂

Three landmarks that capture the multicultural layers of Nagasaki. Sofuku-ji is a Chinese Zen temple from 1629, built by Chinese monks in Ming Dynasty style — the only temple of its kind in Japan. Its Daiippomon gate and main hall are designated National Treasures, distinguished by their vivid vermilion coloring and unmistakably Chinese architectural details.

National Treasures Multicultural Heritage
Sofuku-ji

Founded by Chinese residents of Nagasaki and built entirely in Ming Dynasty Chinese style, this Obaku Zen temple stands apart from every other temple in Japan. The Daiippomon (First Gate) and the Daiyuhoden (Main Hall) are both National Treasures. The temple also houses a massive cauldron that was used to feed victims of the great famine of 1681.

Megane Bridge

Megane-bashi (Spectacles Bridge), built in 1634, is the oldest arched stone bridge in Japan. Its name comes from the way its double arches and their reflection in the Nakashima River below create the shape of a pair of spectacles. A picturesque and much-photographed landmark in central Nagasaki.

Dutch Slope

Oranda-zaka (Dutch Slope) is a historic cobblestone street that once led to the former Dutch and Western enclave. Lined with Western-style Meiji-era buildings, the gently climbing road evokes the atmosphere of Nagasaki’s international past. A pleasant walk that connects to the Glover Garden area.

Advice

These three sites can be combined into a single walking route through central Nagasaki. Together they illustrate the Chinese, Dutch, and Japanese influences that shaped this uniquely cosmopolitan city.

Inasa-yama

稲佐山

Inasa-yama (333m) offers one of the “Three Best Night Views in Japan” alongside Hakodate and Kobe. A cable car carries visitors to the summit for sweeping panoramic views of illuminated Nagasaki, its harbor, and the surrounding islands scattered across the dark sea. The night view is often described as “10 million dollars worth of diamonds.”

Top 3 Night View 333m Summit
Views

From the summit observation deck, the lights of Nagasaki spread out in every direction — tracing the curves of the harbor, the river valleys, and the hillside neighborhoods that give the city its distinctive amphitheater-like topography. On clear nights, distant islands are visible on the horizon.

Cable Car

The Nagasaki Ropeway runs from Fuchi Shrine to the summit, with the ride taking approximately 5 minutes. The cable car operates until approximately 22:00, making evening visits convenient.

Best Time

The most spectacular views come just after dusk, when the city lights begin to appear against the fading twilight sky. Arrive 30 minutes before sunset to experience the full transition from daylight to the glittering night panorama.

Setting

The observation deck features a floor embedded with lights, creating the illusion of standing among the stars. A fitting end to a day spent exploring Nagasaki’s remarkable layers of history and culture.
