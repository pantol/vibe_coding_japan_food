Nikko

日光

Lavish Shrines in Mountain Forest

Overview Attractions
Temple Town

Sacred Splendor in the Mountains

Nikko is a temple town nestled in the mountains of Tochigi Prefecture, approximately two hours north of Tokyo. It is home to Tōshō-gū, the ornately decorated mausoleum of shōgun Tokugawa Ieyasu — the founder of the Tokugawa shogunate that ruled Japan for over 250 years. The shrine complex, set among towering cryptomeria cedars, is a designated UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most lavishly adorned sacred sites in all of Japan.

Where Kyoto’s temples embrace zen simplicity and restrained elegance, Nikko revels in the opposite — every centimeter of Tōshō-gū is covered with intricate carvings, vibrant lacquer, and gold leaf. The effect is overwhelming and deliberate: a display of the Tokugawa clan’s immense power and wealth. Beyond the shrine complex, Nikko’s surrounding mountains offer stunning natural scenery, including Lake Chūzenji and the dramatic Kegon Falls.

The town makes for an excellent day trip from Tokyo, though the combination of shrine visits and the mountain lake area above can easily fill two days. The journey itself — via the Tobu Railway from Asakusa — is a pleasant transition from the capital’s urban sprawl into forested mountain valleys.

Tōshō-gū

東照宮

Mausoleum of shōgun Tokugawa Ieyasu, completed in 1617 and one of the most richly decorated sacred sites in Japan. A UNESCO World Heritage Site where every centimeter is covered with intricate carvings, vibrant lacquer, and gold leaf — a complete contrast to Kyoto’s zen simplicity.

Shinto Shrine UNESCO
Origin

Built in 1617 to enshrine Tokugawa Ieyasu, the founder of the Tokugawa shogunate. Expanded to its current lavish form in 1636 by his grandson Iemitsu, employing over 15,000 craftsmen.

Highlights

Famous carvings include the three wise monkeys — ‘see no evil, hear no evil, speak no evil’ (mizaru, kikazaru, iwazaru) — and the ‘Sleeping Cat’ (Nemuri Neko), attributed to legendary sculptor Hidari Jingoro. The Yomeimon Gate alone features over 500 carvings.

Tips

Hours: 8:00–17:00 (seasonal variations; closes 16:00 in winter). Arrive early to avoid tour groups. The climb to Ieyasu’s tomb at the top is steep but rewarding.

Where

2301 Sannai, Nikko — a 30-minute walk or short bus ride from Nikko Station

Rinnō-ji

輪王寺

The most important Buddhist temple in Nikko, housing three magnificent 8-meter golden statues representing Amida Buddha, Senju Kannon (Thousand-Armed Kannon), and Bato Kannon (Horse-Headed Kannon). Each deity corresponds to one of the three sacred mountains of Nikko.

Buddhist Temple UNESCO
Origin

Founded in the 8th century by the monk Shodo Shonin, who first brought Buddhism to the Nikko mountains. The temple grew in prominence alongside Tōshō-gū.

Highlights

The Sanbutsudo (Three Buddha Hall) is the largest building in Nikko and contains the three towering gilded statues. The Shōyō-en garden is a beautiful Edo-period strolling garden.

Tips

Easily visited in combination with Tōshō-gū as the two sites are adjacent. Separate admission required.

Where

2300 Sannai, Nikko — immediately adjacent to Tōshō-gū

Futarasan-jinja

二荒山神社

One of the oldest buildings in the Nikko region, this Shinto shrine is dedicated to the deities of Nikko’s three sacred mountains. Its serene, moss-covered grounds offer a peaceful contrast to the exuberance of neighboring Tōshō-gū.

Shinto Shrine UNESCO
Origin

Originally established in 767 by the monk Shodo Shonin, making it far older than Tōshō-gū. The shrine is dedicated to the kami of Mount Nantai, Mount Nyoho, and Mount Taro.

Highlights

The tranquil, forested grounds feel a world apart from the ornate Tōshō-gū next door. The shrine also oversees the Shinkyo Bridge and a separate branch shrine at Lake Chūzenji.

Tips

Often overlooked by visitors rushing between Tōshō-gū and Rinnō-ji, but well worth a quiet visit. Included in the UNESCO World Heritage designation.

Where

2307 Sannai, Nikko — within the shrine-temple complex, next to Tōshō-gū

Shinkyo Bridge

神橋

The Sacred Bridge spanning the Daiya River gorge, considered one of the three most beautiful bridges in Japan. Its vivid vermilion lacquer arches dramatically over the rushing river against a backdrop of forested mountains.

Historic Landmark UNESCO
Origin

According to legend, the monk Shodo Shonin was trying to cross the Daiya River in 766 when the god Jinja-Daiou summoned two serpents to form a bridge. The current structure dates from 1636.

Highlights

One of Japan’s three finest bridges (Nihon Sankyō). The bridge is particularly photogenic in autumn when surrounded by blazing red and gold foliage, and in winter with a dusting of snow.

Tips

You can cross the bridge for a small fee, or photograph it for free from the adjacent road bridge. Located at the entrance to the shrine area, making it a natural starting point for a visit.

Where

At the entrance to the Nikko shrine complex, a 20-minute walk from Nikko Station

Lake Chūzenji & Kegon Falls

中禅寺湖・華厳の滝

A scenic mountain lake above Nikko reached via the winding Irohazaka switchback road, paired with Kegon Falls — a dramatic 97-meter cascade and one of Japan’s most famous waterfalls. The area is spectacular during autumn foliage season.

Natural Scenery Elevator: ¥570
Origin

Lake Chūzenji was formed around 20,000 years ago by volcanic activity from Mount Nantai, which dammed the valley. Kegon Falls is the lake’s primary outlet.

Highlights

Take the elevator (¥570) down to the observation platform at the base of Kegon Falls for a dramatic close-up view. The lake shore offers pleasant walks and boat cruises. In winter, the falls partially freeze into spectacular ice formations.

Tips

Located about 30 minutes by bus from central Nikko, up the famous Irohazaka switchback road (48 hairpin turns). Combine with the shrines for a full day, or dedicate a separate day if time allows.

Where

Chūgūshi, Nikko — take the Tobu bus from Nikko Station (~50 min) or central Nikko (~30 min)

Getting There

Nikko is approximately 2 hours from Tokyo via the Tobu Railway from Asakusa Station. The Limited Express (Spacia) is the most comfortable option. Plan a full day to cover both the shrine complex and the Lake Chūzenji area, or consider an overnight stay to explore at a relaxed pace.
