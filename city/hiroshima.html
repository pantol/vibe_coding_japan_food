City of Peace

City of Peace and Resilience

On the morning of August 6, 1945, the atomic bomb “Little Boy” was dropped on Hiroshima, devastating the city and changing the course of human history forever. In the decades that followed, Hiroshima rose from the ashes with a singular purpose: to become a living monument to peace and a warning against the horrors of nuclear warfare. The city’s reconstruction stands as one of the most remarkable acts of collective resilience in the modern era.

Today, Hiroshima is a vibrant, forward-looking city that carries its past with dignity and determination. The Peace Memorial Park, situated at the epicenter of the blast, draws millions of visitors each year who come to pay their respects, learn from survivor testimonies, and reflect on the importance of peace. The Atomic Bomb Dome — the skeletal remains of the former Prefectural Industrial Promotion Hall — stands as a UNESCO World Heritage Site and the most powerful symbol of both destruction and hope.

Beyond its memorial sites, Hiroshima is a city of rivers, bridges, and green spaces, with a warm and welcoming spirit that belies its tragic history. Its transformation from ground zero to a global beacon of peace education and nuclear disarmament advocacy is a testament to the enduring strength of the human will to rebuild, remember, and work toward a better future.

Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum & Atomic Bomb Dome

広島平和記念資料館 & 原爆ドーム

On August 6, 1945 at 8:15 AM, the atomic bomb “Little Boy” exploded approximately 600 meters above central Hiroshima, killing an estimated 80,000 people instantly — a figure that rose to 140,000 by the end of the year. The Genbaku Dome (Atomic Bomb Dome) is the only building near the hypocenter that partially survived the blast; its exposed steel dome and crumbling walls remain standing today as a haunting Peace Monument and UNESCO World Heritage Site.

UNESCO World Heritage Peace Memorial
Museum

The Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum chronicles the events of August 6 through personal belongings, photographs, and first-hand accounts from survivors (hibakusha). The newly renovated main exhibition shifts focus to individual human stories, making the experience deeply personal and profoundly moving.

Park

The surrounding Peace Memorial Park includes the Cenotaph bearing the names of all known victims, the Peace Bell that visitors may ring, and the Children’s Peace Monument — inspired by Sadako Sasaki and the legend of folding 1,000 origami cranes for a wish of healing.

Hours

Museum open 7:30–19:00 (hours vary by season). Reserve at least 2 hours for the museum. The Atomic Bomb Dome is visible 24 hours a day from the surrounding park and riverbank.

Advice

Prepare yourself emotionally. The exhibitions contain graphic and deeply affecting material. The experience is essential but heavy. Consider visiting the garden or riverside afterwards for quiet reflection.

Shukkeien Garden

縮景園

A historic Japanese strolling garden dating to 1620, designed by tea ceremony master Ueda Soko for the feudal lord (daimyō) Asano Nagaakira. The garden miniaturizes famous Chinese and Japanese landscapes around a tranquil central pond, creating an entire world in miniature.

Historic Garden Est. 1620
History

Nearly completely destroyed by the atomic bomb in 1945, Shukkeien was painstakingly rebuilt and reopened in 1951. Its recovery mirrors the resilience of Hiroshima itself, transforming from ruins back into a place of beauty and contemplation.

Features

The garden offers traditional tea houses where visitors can enjoy matcha, elegant arched bridges crossing the central pond, and meticulously maintained seasonal plantings — cherry blossoms in spring, irises in early summer, autumn foliage, and plum blossoms in winter.

Hours

Open daily 9:00–17:00. A peaceful retreat just a short walk from the Peace Memorial Park, ideal for quiet reflection after visiting the museum.

Setting

A serene haven in the heart of the city. Walking its winding paths around the central pond, past miniature mountains and valleys, feels like stepping into a landscape painting come to life.
