The Hells

Seven Hells of Beppu

Beppu’s famous “hells” (jigoku) are seven spectacular hot springs where water reaches temperatures of nearly 100°C — far too hot for bathing, but mesmerizing to witness. Each hell has its own distinct character: cobalt-blue pools, blood-red waters, erupting geysers, and steaming mud pits that seem pulled from another world entirely.

For over 1,000 years, the Kannawa area where most of the hells are concentrated was avoided by locals who considered the bubbling, steaming earth to be cursed ground. The relentless volcanic activity made the land uninhabitable and deeply unsettling to those who lived nearby. It was only in the modern era that the hells were reimagined as natural wonders rather than supernatural threats.

Today, the seven hells are protected as Places of Special Scenic Beauty by the Japanese government — a designation reserved for sites of exceptional natural value. They remain among the most extraordinary geothermal landscapes on earth, drawing visitors from around the world to witness the raw power of Beppu’s volcanic heart.

Beppu Jigoku (Hells of Beppu)

別府地獄

Seven spectacular “hells” — geothermal hot springs too hot for bathing but extraordinary to behold. From cobalt-blue pools and blood-red ponds to erupting geysers and crocodile-inhabited waters, each hell offers a unique window into Beppu’s volcanic soul.

Geothermal Wonder ¥2,400
Umi Jigoku (Sea Hell)

A stunning cobalt-blue pool heated to 98°C and reaching 200 meters deep. The vivid color comes from dissolved iron sulfate. The grounds are surrounded by giant Amazonian water lilies — Victoria amazonica — that thrive in the naturally heated water. The most beautiful and photogenic of all seven hells.

Chinoike Jigoku (Blood Pond Hell)

The oldest natural hell in Japan, with blood-red water created by iron oxide and magnesium rising from deep underground. The crimson clay that settles at the bottom has been used in traditional skin ointments for centuries. An eerie, unforgettable sight.

Tatsumaki Jigoku (Tornado Hell)

The only geyser among the seven hells, erupting faithfully every 30–40 minutes. Without the stone roof constructed above it, the boiling water column would reach over 30 meters into the air. The eruptions last several minutes and are accompanied by a deep, rumbling roar.

Oniyama Jigoku (Demon Mountain Hell)

Home to over 80 crocodiles bred in the naturally hot water for more than 100 years. The geothermal heat keeps the enclosures at tropical temperatures year-round, making it one of the most unusual crocodile habitats in the world.

Kamado Jigoku (Cooking Pot Hell)

The most interactive of the seven hells, featuring six mini-hells with different colors and temperatures. Visitors can enjoy steam shows, watch demonstrations, and try food cooked entirely by geothermal steam. The jigoku mushi (hell-steaming food) tradition dates back to the Edo period.

What to Try

Eggs cooked in the boiling springs — the mineral water gives them a distinctive flavor and texture. The legendary pudding from Umi Jigoku, gently steamed by volcanic heat, is rich, silky, and unlike any custard you’ve had before.

Practical Info

Hours: 8:00–17:00 daily. All-seven-hells ticket: approximately ¥2,400. Five of the hells are clustered together in the Kannawa district and can be walked between. The remaining two (Chinoike and Tatsumaki) are located in Shibaseki, about 3 km away — a short bus ride or taxi.

Mount Tsurumi (Beppu Ropeway)

鶴見岳

A cable car ascent to the summit of Mount Tsurumi at 1,375 meters, offering panoramic views of Beppu city, the bay, and the surrounding volcanic mountains. On clear days, the distant shores of Shikoku are visible across the Inland Sea.

Scenic Viewpoint 1,375m
The Ride

The Beppu Ropeway carries passengers from the base station to the summit of Mount Tsurumi in approximately 10 minutes. The cable car ascends smoothly over forested slopes, with the cityscape of Beppu and the glittering bay gradually unfolding below.

At the Summit

A viewpoint platform and short walking trails await at the top. The 360-degree panorama takes in Beppu’s steaming onsen districts, the crescent of Beppu Bay, and the green ridgelines of the surrounding Kuju mountain range. On clear days, you can see all the way across the Inland Sea to the island of Shikoku.

Best Time

Early morning for the clearest views. Autumn brings spectacular foliage on the mountainside, and winter occasionally dusts the summit with snow — a striking contrast to the steaming city below.
