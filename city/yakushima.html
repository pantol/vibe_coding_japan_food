Yakushima

屋久島

The Ancient Cedar Forest

Overview Attractions
Attraction Guide

The Primeval Island

Yakushima is a roughly circular, mountainous island off the southern tip of Kyushu in Kagoshima Prefecture — a place where approximately 90% of the land is covered in dense forest. In 1993, it became one of Japan’s first UNESCO World Heritage Sites, recognized for the outstanding universal value of its ancient temperate rainforest ecosystem.

The island is home to the legendary yakusugi — ancient Japanese cedar trees (Cryptomeria japonica) over 1,000 years old. The oldest of these, the Jōmon Sugi, is estimated to be at least 2,200 years old, with some estimates reaching several thousand years more. These towering, gnarled giants grow in a landscape of moss-draped ravines, cascading waterfalls, and mist-shrouded peaks.

Yakushima is famously known as the inspiration for the enchanted forest in Hayao Miyazaki’s Studio Ghibli masterpiece Princess Mononoke (1997). The island’s otherworldly atmosphere — where ancient trees emerge from carpets of luminous green moss and clouds drift through the canopy — makes it one of the most extraordinary natural destinations in all of Japan.

Attractions

Yakusugi Museum (Yakusugi Shizenkan)

屋久杉自然館

An excellent introduction to Yakushima’s ecology and history. The museum traces the island’s forestry history, explaining how during the Edo period yakusugi trees were massively logged and exported because their exceptionally high resin content makes the wood remarkably rot-resistant. Exhibits clearly explain the difference between regular sugi (Japanese cedar) and yakusugi — trees over 1,000 years old.

Museum & Education Visit Before Trekking
Why Visit First

The museum provides essential context for understanding the ancient forests you’ll trek through. Learning about the ecology, the logging history, and the significance of the yakusugi before seeing them in person transforms the hiking experience. Visit BEFORE trekking to Jōmon Sugi for the fullest appreciation.

Trailhead Access

The bus to the Jōmon Sugi trail (Arakawa trailhead) departs from directly in front of the museum. During the March–November restricted access period, this is the mandatory departure point for hikers, making it a natural first stop before heading into the forest.

Shiratani Unsuikyō

白谷雲水峡

A ravine and primeval forest park that inspired the enchanted forest in Studio Ghibli’s Princess Mononoke (1997). Lead background artist Oga Kazuo spent hours sketching the moss-covered trees and stones of this forest. Home to over 600 species of moss, recognized by the Japanese Bryological Society as a “Precious Moss Forest of Japan.”

Primeval Forest Donation Entry
Trail Routes

Three routes of varying length and difficulty: the Yayoi-sugi course (approximately 1 hour), the Bugyosugi course (approximately 3 hours), and the Taikoiwa Round Trip (4–5 hours). The trail to Kokemusu-no-mori (Moss Forest) — the actual location that inspired the Mononoke forest — leads through a fairytale landscape of luminous green moss draped over ancient trees and boulders.

Taikoiwa

The longest trail leads to Taikoiwa rock, a massive granite outcrop offering panoramic views over the forest canopy and out to the coast. The climb is rewarding but demanding — allow 4–5 hours for the full round trip.

Getting There

Located approximately 30 minutes by car from Miyanoura port. Buses run every 2–3 hours. Entry is by voluntary donation (conservation contribution). Bring rain gear — Yakushima receives extraordinary amounts of rainfall, and the forest is at its most magical when mist drifts through the canopy.

Jōmon Sugi

縄文杉

The oldest and thickest known Cryptomeria japonica tree in the world. With a trunk circumference of 16.4 meters, the Jōmon Sugi has an estimated age of 2,200–7,200 years — most experts agree it has been growing for at least several thousand years, since the prehistoric Jōmon period, hence its name. Rediscovered in 1966, the find led to the end of logging in the Arakawa region by 1970.

Ancient Cedar ~22km / 9–10 hours
The Trail

The trail from the Arakawa trailhead is approximately 22 km round trip, requiring 9–10 hours of hiking. The first 9 km follow old forestry railway tracks — relatively flat and easy walking. After that, the Okabu trail turns to steep wooden stairs, root networks, and increasingly demanding terrain as you climb deeper into the ancient forest.

Notable Trees

The trail passes several remarkable trees: Sandai-sugi (38.4 meters tall), Wilson Stump (a huge stump you can enter and look up through to see a famous heart-shaped opening in the canopy), Meoto-sugi (the “married couple” trees joined together by a branch), and Daio-sugi (the “King of Cedars”). Jōmon Sugi itself is viewed from a platform approximately 15 meters away to protect its root system.

Access Restrictions

IMPORTANT: From March through November, private cars cannot reach the Arakawa trailhead. You must take the designated bus from Yakusugi Museum. Start your hike between 5:00 and 7:00 AM to complete the trail in daylight. This is a serious day hike — proper preparation is essential.

What to Bring

Bring: packed food and water (no shops on the trail), rain gear (essential — Yakushima is one of the wettest places in Japan), a flashlight or headlamp, and sturdy hiking boots. Toilets are available at the trailhead, near Sandai-sugi, at the Okabu trail junction, and at Takatsuka Hut.

Yakusugi Land

ヤクスギランド

A primeval forest park offering several trails ranging from 30 minutes to 2.5 hours, significantly less demanding than Shiratani Unsuikyō or the Jōmon Sugi trek. Wooden boardwalks provide accessible routes through stands of ancient yakusugi cedars, making it an excellent option for visitors of all fitness levels.

Forest Park 30 min – 2.5 hours
Trails

The most popular trail takes approximately 2 hours and winds through ancient forest on well-maintained boardwalks. Shorter 30-minute loops are available for those with limited time. The park provides a good complement to the main trails, especially if weather conditions limit longer treks.

Getting There

Located approximately 30 minutes by car from most accommodation areas on the island. The park offers a more relaxed way to experience old-growth yakusugi forest without committing to a full-day expedition.
