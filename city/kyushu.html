Chapter 7

A Regional Specialties Overview

Beyond Fukuoka and Oita, the prefectures of Saga, Nagasaki, Kumamoto, Miyazaki, and Kagoshima form a culinary region unlike any other in Japan. Sweet soy sauce (amakuchi shoyu) grows sweeter the farther south you travel — a legacy of Nagasaki’s role as Japan’s sole open port during Edo-era isolation, when Dutch and Portuguese traders imported sugar through the artificial island of Dejima. That same trade channel brought tempura, castella cake, and konpeitō candy to Japan.

Kyushu is Japan’s shochu heartland, with nearly 500 distilleries. Three production areas hold WTO Geographical Indication status — equivalent to Scotch or Champagne: Satsuma (Kagoshima), Kuma (Kumamoto), and Iki (Nagasaki). The warmer subtropical climate favors robust livestock and year-round seafood, while volcanic soil from Mt. Aso and Sakurajima produces the sweet potatoes that are the foundation of the region’s great spirit.

The coastline yields extraordinary seafood from three bodies of water — the Sea of Japan, the Pacific, and the East China Sea. The island’s identity is built on the centrality of pork (especially Kagoshima’s kurobuta), the prominence of shochu over sake, and a warmth that extends to its people and their cooking.

Street Food & Markets

Where the Region Eats

Nagasaki Dejima Wharf

Waterfront complex on Nagasaki harbor with seafood restaurants and market stalls. Fresh catches from the East China Sea, champon noodle shops, and views of the bay. A gathering point for Nagasaki's culinary heritage.

Kagoshima Tenmonkan

Kagoshima's central covered arcade and the heart of the city's street food scene. Satsuma-age, Shirokuma shaved ice, kurobuta skewers, and shochu bars line the bustling pedestrian streets. Best experienced in the early evening.

Kumamoto Shimotori Arcade

Kumamoto's main shopping arcade with basashi stalls, local izakayas, and specialty food shops. Horse meat sashimi is the star, but you'll also find karashi renkon (mustard-stuffed lotus root) and other Kumamoto delicacies.

Yobuko Morning Market

One of Japan's top three morning markets, located in Saga prefecture. Famous for live squid sliced to order — so fresh it's still moving on the plate. Arrive before 10 a.m. for the full experience.

Local Drinks

What to Drink

🍶

Satsuma Shochu

Sweet potato shochu from Kagoshima — the soul of Kyushu drinking culture. Try Kuro Kirishima or other imo-jochu on the rocks or with hot water (oyuwari).

🍶

Mugi Shochu

Barley-based shochu, lighter and smoother than its sweet potato cousin. Popular across northern Kyushu, especially in Oita and Fukuoka.

🍺

Kyushu Craft Beers

Growing craft beer scene with breweries like Kirishima Brewery. Local ingredients and volcanic water contribute to distinctive flavors.

🍵

Ryukyu Black Tea

Subtropical black tea from southern Kyushu. Rich and malty, a unique alternative to green tea in the warmer climate.

🍋

Yakushima Lemon Liqueur

Citrus liqueur from Yakushima island, made with local lemons. Refreshing and aromatic, a taste of Kyushu's subtropical character.

Dining Etiquette

Rules of the Table

Warm & Relaxed

Kyushu dining is friendly and relaxed. Communal tables and open seats are common. Chefs appreciate conversation — don't be afraid to engage.

Shochu Sharing

Shochu bottles are shared family-style. Pour for others before yourself. Ordering a bottle for the table is the Kyushu way.

Noodle Etiquette

Slurping is acceptable and encouraged with champon and ramen. Eat promptly while hot. Noodles wait for no one.

Showing Respect

Show respect with a bow and say "sumimasen" when leaving. Kyushu hospitality is warm — reciprocate with gratitude and good manners.
