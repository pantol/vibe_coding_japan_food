Takachiho

高千穂

Where Gods Descended from Heaven

Overview Attractions
Attraction Guide

Land of Gods and Gorges

Takachiho is a small town in the mountainous interior of Miyazaki Prefecture, Kyushu — a place of deep mythological significance where, according to Japanese mythology, the deity Ninigi-no-Mikoto descended from the heavens to rule Japan. This act, known as tenson kōrin, marks the spiritual origin of the Japanese imperial line and anchors Takachiho as one of the most sacred landscapes in all of Shinto tradition.

Beyond its mythology, Takachiho is home to a spectacular gorge carved through ancient volcanic basalt, where sheer cliff walls frame emerald waters and a thundering waterfall. Sacred caves, ancient shrines, and nightly kagura dance performances connect visitors to a living spiritual heritage that stretches back millennia.

Remote and unhurried, Takachiho rewards those willing to venture off the beaten path. The combination of otherworldly natural beauty and profound cultural significance makes it one of the most memorable destinations in southern Japan.

Takachiho Gorge (Takachiho-kyō)

高千穂峡

A spectacular gorge carved by the Gokase River through ancient basalt lava layers. Cliff walls reach nearly 100 meters high, adorned with surreal columnar jointing rock formations. The 17-meter Manai Waterfall — one of Japan’s 100 Best Waterfalls — cascades into emerald water below.

Natural Wonder ¥4,100–5,100 / boat
Experience

The most iconic experience is the 30-minute rowboat ride that takes you directly under the Manai Waterfall. Gliding through the narrow gorge with towering basalt columns on either side and mist from the falls drifting over you is unforgettable. Boats operate 8:30–17:00. Cost is approximately ¥4,100–5,100 per boat (up to 3 people). Book online in advance — weekend reservations are required.

Mythology

In Japanese mythology, this is where Ninigi-no-Mikoto descended from heaven to earth. Nearby Amano-Iwato cave is where the sun goddess Amaterasu hid herself, plunging the world into darkness. The other deities staged a wild, raucous dance to lure her out — this is the origin of kagura, the sacred dances still performed in Takachiho today.

Kagura

Nightly Takachiho Kagura performances are held at Takachiho Shrine from 20:00 to 21:00. These sacred dances re-enact scenes from Japanese mythology and have been performed for centuries. A rare opportunity to witness living Shinto tradition.

Visiting

The gorge walking trail runs approximately 1 km along the river. Best visited in early morning to avoid crowds, especially during autumn foliage season. The gorge is beautifully lit at night during certain seasons.

Mount Aso

阿蘇山

One of the world’s largest active volcanoes, with a caldera circumference of approximately 120 km — among the largest on earth. The main crater, Nakadake, is one of the few active craters in the world that visitors can approach when conditions allow.

Active Volcano Check Status Before Visiting
The Crater

Nakadake’s turquoise acidic lake changes color based on volcanic activity, shifting between vivid blue, green, and grey. Standing at the crater rim and peering down into the steaming, otherworldly lake is a humbling experience. Access may be closed during periods of heightened volcanic alerts — always check the current volcano status before visiting.

Kusasenri

Inside the vast caldera lies Kusasenri, an expanse of rolling grassy plains used for horse and cattle grazing. The surreal contrast between peaceful pastoral meadows and the smoking volcanic crater just beyond is unlike anything else in Japan.

Scale

The Aso caldera is so enormous that entire towns, farms, and railway lines exist inside it. The caldera was formed by a series of massive eruptions between 270,000 and 90,000 years ago. Today, around 50,000 people live within the caldera walls.

Visiting

The Aso Volcano Museum and ropeway provide access to the crater area. Check the Japan Meteorological Agency’s volcanic alert level before planning your visit. When the alert level is 2 or above, access to the crater is restricted. The surrounding grasslands and outer caldera rim remain accessible year-round.
