Yoshinogari

吉野ヶ里

Window to Prehistoric Japan

Overview Attractions

Japan’s Ancient Settlement

Yoshinogari is the largest archaeological site from the Yayoi period (300 BCE – 300 CE) in Japan, located in Saga Prefecture on the island of Kyushu. Discovered in 1986, the excavations revealed the remarkably preserved remains of a large, fortified settlement surrounded by moats and wooden palisades — offering an unparalleled glimpse into the lives of Japan’s earliest organized communities.

The scale and sophistication of the settlement have led some researchers to connect Yoshinogari to the legendary kingdom of Yamatai, said to have been ruled by the enigmatic Queen Himiko in the 3rd century. While the identification remains debated among scholars, the site’s defensive structures, burial mounds, and evidence of social hierarchy align strikingly with Chinese historical accounts of Himiko’s realm.

Today, the site has been transformed into a sprawling historical park where reconstructed buildings, watchtowers, and storehouses bring the Yayoi world vividly to life. Yoshinogari stands as a rare and tangible connection to an era long before the emergence of the Japanese state as we know it.

Yoshinogari Historical Park

吉野ヶ里歴史公園

A vast open-air park built over the original archaeological site, featuring faithful reconstructions of Yayoi-period buildings, watchtowers, and storehouses that show how ancient Japanese people lived long before the state existed. The park also offers hands-on workshops in traditional crafts such as ceramics and weaving.

Historical Park ~2h visit
The Settlement

The reconstructed settlement includes pit dwellings, elevated storehouses, and imposing watchtowers that once guarded the perimeter. Walking through the site, visitors can enter reconstructed buildings and see how the Yayoi people organized their community — from the inner sanctum where leaders gathered to the outer moats and palisades that protected them from rival clans.

Workshops

The park offers hands-on workshops where visitors can try traditional Yayoi-era crafts, including pottery making using ancient techniques, weaving on replica looms, and fire-starting with friction tools. These activities provide a tactile connection to the daily life of people who inhabited this site over two thousand years ago.

Visiting

Allow approximately two hours to explore the park thoroughly. The site is expansive, covering a large area of reconstructed zones, exhibition halls, and open grounds. Comfortable walking shoes are recommended. The park is accessible by train from Saga city and makes an excellent half-day excursion.
