Chapter 3

A Thousand Years of Imperial and Monastic Culinary Tradition

Kyoto’s cuisine, Kyo-ryori, is among the world’s most refined — shaped by over a millennium as Japan’s imperial capital and codified into five distinct styles: imperial court cuisine, aristocratic palace-style, samurai banquet, Buddhist temple vegetarian (shojin ryori), and the tea-ceremony kaiseki that evolved into Japan’s highest culinary art. Buddhist shojin ryori arrived from China in the 6th century and was codified by Zen patriarch Dogen in the 13th; Sen no Rikyū reimagined it in the 15th century as cha-kaiseki. Kyoto ranks third globally for Michelin stars, with 469 recommended establishments in the 2025 guide.

Two elements underpin everything: Kyo-yasai — roughly 32 heirloom vegetable varieties (Kamo eggplant, Shogoin daikon, Kujo green onions) cultivated in Kyoto’s fertile basin — and the city’s pristine underground springs. The water in Fushimi, known as “Fushimizu,” is the foundation of Kyoto’s extraordinary tofu, yuba, sake, and dashi.

Everything in Kyo-ryori serves a single principle: minimal seasoning to showcase the natural flavor of ingredients at their seasonal peak. The word “kaiseki” literally means “breast-pocket stone,” from monks who warmed stones against their stomachs to suppress hunger — a reminder that the cuisine was born from deprivation, not abundance.

Kaiseki Ryori

懐石料理

Philosophy made edible. Rooted in Sen no Rikyu's tea ceremony, kaiseki presents 7–14 courses that mirror the seasons. Each dish is gentle, umami-rich, and presented with artistic precision.

Signature Dish ¥¥¥¥
Origin

Rooted in the tea ceremony tradition of Sen no Rikyu, refined over centuries in Kyoto

Flavor

Gentle, umami-rich courses with artistic presentation. Each dish reflects the current season.

Setting

Intimate tatami rooms in ryotei restaurants. Multi-course, meditative dining.

Where

Hyotei, Nakamura, Gion Sasaki

Yudofu

湯豆腐

Simmered tofu, Kyoto simplicity at its purest. Cubes of silken tofu gently heated in kombu-dashi, eaten with ponzu or sesame sauce. Temple restaurants near Nanzen-ji perfected this dish.

Temple Cuisine ¥
Origin

Buddhist temple cuisine, refined by tofu shops near Nanzen-ji temple

Flavor

Delicate, clean, and warming. The purity of Kyoto's soft water shines through the silken tofu.

Setting

Temple restaurants near Nanzen-ji, often with garden views. Best in winter.

Where

Yudofu Sagano

Yuba

湯葉

Tofu skin with delicate texture and high protein. A mild, subtle soya flavor that rewards attention. Found fresh at tofu shops near Kiyomizu-dera or as a refined element in upscale kaiseki.

Kyoto Specialty ¥¥
Origin

Traditional tofu skin, central to Kyoto's temple and vegetarian cuisine

Flavor

Mild, subtle soya flavor with a delicate, silky texture. High in protein.

Setting

Fresh at tofu shops near Kiyomizu-dera or served in upscale kaiseki courses.

Obanzai Ryori

おばんざい

Traditional Kyoto home-style cooking. Simple seasonal vegetable dishes, light soups, and simmered greens served at small "obanzaiya" restaurants throughout the city.

Home-style ¥¥

Traditional Kyoto home cooking, passed down through generations of local households

Flavor

Simple, seasonal vegetable dishes with light dashi. Simmered greens, light soups, and pickled vegetables.

Setting

Nishin Soba

にしんそば

Buckwheat noodles topped with sweet-soy marinated herring. An old Kyoto specialty combining earthy soba in light dashi with rich, savory preserved fish.

Old Kyoto ¥
Origin

Old Kyoto specialty, born from the inland city's tradition of preserved seafood

Flavor

Earthy buckwheat noodles in light dashi, topped with sweet-soy marinated herring. Savory and comforting.

Setting

Traditional soba shops in the old city center.

Yatsuhashi

八つ橋

Sweet rice flour confection dating to the Edo period. Thin mochi-like sheets flavored with cinnamon or matcha. Kyoto's most famous souvenir sweet, found at every station and temple gate.

Sweet / Souvenir ¥
Origin

Edo-period confection, named after the koto musician Yatsuhashi Kengyō

Flavor

Thin, mochi-like rice flour sheets with cinnamon or matcha. Soft, chewy, and subtly sweet.

Setting

Souvenir shops at Kyoto Station, temple gates, and Nishiki Market.

Street Food & Markets

Where the City Eats

Nishiki Market

Known as the "Kitchen of Kyoto," this covered arcade has operated since the 14th century. Narrow lanes lined with over a hundred vendors selling pickled vegetables (world-class tsukemono), fresh tofu, yuba, grilled seafood, and seasonal sweets. Go late morning for the best selection.

Gion Street Shops

Along the atmospheric streets of Gion, small vendors sell yakitori, wagashi (traditional sweets), and seasonal treats. The perfect stop between temple visits, offering a taste of Kyoto's refined street food culture amid the historic geisha district.

Local Drinks

What to Drink

🍵

Uji Matcha

Kyoto's Uji district produces Japan's finest matcha. Experience it in a traditional tea ceremony or as a latte in modern kissaten. The ceremonial grade is intensely umami and smooth.

🍶

Fushimi Sake

Fushimi's soft water produces sake that is gentle, sweet, and round. Visit the Gekkeikan Okura Sake Museum or Kizakura Kappa Country for tastings and history.

🍊

Yuzu-Citrus Liqueur

Fragrant yuzu-infused liqueurs are a Kyoto specialty. Bright, tart, and floral — perfect as a digestif or mixed with soda water in summer.

🍑

Ume (Plum) Liqueur

Umeshu made with locally grown ume plums. Sweet, tart, and aromatic. Served on the rocks or with soda at izakayas throughout the city.

Sightseeing

Must-See Attractions

Click any attraction to learn more about its history, significance, and visitor tips.

Nijo-jo (Nijo Castle)

二条城

Built in 1603 by shogun Tokugawa Ieyasu as his official Kyoto residence. UNESCO World Heritage site famous for nightingale floors (uguisubari) that chirp underfoot to detect intruders.

UNESCO Heritage Hours: 8:45–17:00
History

Ninomaru Palace features stunning Kano school paintings on gold leaf screens. In 1867, the last shogun Tokugawa Yoshinobu returned governing power to Emperor Meiji in this very hall, ending over 250 years of feudal rule in Japan.

Gardens

Designed by master landscape architect Kobori Enshu. Multiple garden styles across the sprawling castle grounds.

Tip

Walk slowly on the nightingale floors to hear the distinctive chirping sound created by metal clamps beneath the boards.

Fushimi Inari-Taisha

伏見稲荷大社

The most important of over 30,000 Inari shrines in Japan. Founded in 711 and famous for its thousands of orange-red torii gates forming tunnels up Mt Inari (233m).

Shinto Shrine Free 24/7
The Trail

The full trail is approximately 4km and takes 2–3 hours to complete. Thousands of torii gates, donated by businesses and individuals, line the path — a single gate starts at around ¥400,000.

Symbolism

Fox (kitsune) statues throughout the shrine hold keys, jewels, and scrolls — messengers of the god Inari. The most visited tourist attraction in all of Japan.

Tip

Visit early morning or late evening to avoid crowds. The shrine is open 24 hours and the gates are illuminated at night.

Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion)

金閣寺

Upper floors covered in real gold leaf. Originally built in 1397 for retired shogun Ashikaga Yoshimitsu. Three floors showcase three distinct architectural styles.

UNESCO Heritage Hours: 9:00–17:00 (~¥500)
History

Deliberately set on fire in 1950 by a young monk — an event that inspired Yukio Mishima’s celebrated novel “The Temple of the Golden Pavilion.” Rebuilt in 1955 even more richly gilded than the original.

Architecture

Three floors in three styles: shinden (palace style) on the ground floor, buke (samurai style) on the second, and zen (Chinese style) on the top. A golden phoenix on the roof symbolizes rebirth.

Tip

The reflection in the mirror pond is iconic — visit on a clear day for the best photographs.

Arashiyama & Bamboo Grove

嵐山

Famous bamboo grove with towering stalks reaching 30 meters. The Togetsukyo Bridge dates from the Heian period, and the surrounding district is rich with temples and gardens.

Historic District Tenryu-ji: 8:30–17:00
Tenryu-ji

Founded in 1339, it is the main temple of the Rinzai zen school. Contains Sogenchi, one of the oldest gardens in Japan, designed by the legendary Muso Soseki.

Gioji Temple

A hidden temple nestled in a bamboo and maple grove, its grounds blanketed in moss. Connected to a tragic love story from the Heike Monogatari epic.

Tip

Arrive at the bamboo grove before 8:00 AM to experience it without crowds. Combine with a visit to Tenryu-ji and the monkey park.

Kiyomizu-dera

清水寺

Founded in 778. The main hall stands on a massive wooden platform supported by 139 pillars, jutting 13 meters from the hillside — built entirely without a single nail.

UNESCO Heritage Hours: 6:00–18:00
Significance

The Japanese saying “jumping from Kiyomizu’s stage” means taking a bold, life-changing decision — a testament to the temple’s dramatic cliffside presence.

Otowa Waterfall

Three streams pour into a basin below the main hall: one for long life, one for success in studies, and one for love. Tradition says you must choose only one — drinking from all three is considered greedy.

Tip

Visit at dawn when the temple opens at 6:00 AM for a peaceful experience. The approach streets are lined with pottery and souvenir shops.

Gion

祇園

Japan’s most famous geisha (geiko/maiko) district. The atmospheric streets of Hanamikoji are lined with traditional tea houses (ochaya) and maintain strict regulations to preserve their historic character.

Historic District Free
Culture

Geiko (Kyoto’s term for geisha) and maiko (apprentice geiko) still practice traditional arts here. Gion Corner offers daily shows of tea ceremony, flower arrangement, court music, and traditional dance.

Atmosphere

Hanamikoji street is the heart of the district, with wooden machiya townhouses converted into exclusive ochaya tea houses. Strict regulations maintain the traditional appearance of every building.

Tip

Be respectful of geiko and maiko — do not block their path or take photos without permission. Early evening is the best time to spot them heading to engagements.

Dining Etiquette

Rules of the Table

Formal Kaiseki Meals

Formal meals are served on tatami. Remove shoes at the entrance. Sit in seiza or as directed. Follow the pace of the courses — never rush or rearrange the presentation.

Chopstick Etiquette

Never stick chopsticks upright in rice — this resembles a funeral rite. Wait for the eldest person to begin eating. Say "itadakimasu" before the first bite.

At the Ryotei

Tipping is strictly taboo at traditional ryotei restaurants. The service charge is included. Express gratitude verbally with "gochisosama deshita" when leaving.

Tea House Etiquette

Punctuality is essential at tea houses — late arrivals may be refused entry. Follow the host's guidance. Rotate the tea bowl before drinking. Admire the wagashi before eating it.
