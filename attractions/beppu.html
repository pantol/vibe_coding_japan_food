Onsen Paradise

Japan’s Hot Spring Capital

Beppu is the second largest hot spring source in the world, surpassed only by Yellowstone in the United States. Nestled between the mountains and the sea on the eastern coast of Kyushu, this extraordinary city is home to over 2,800 hot spring vents that pump out nearly 130,000 tons of steaming thermal water every single day. Plumes of steam rise from streets, hillsides, and drainage grates, giving the city an otherworldly, almost primordial atmosphere.

For over a thousand years, the geothermal areas of Beppu were feared and avoided — locals called them “jigoku” (hells), believing the boiling, vividly colored pools were cursed gateways to the underworld. Today, seven of these hells have been developed into one of Japan’s most unique and unforgettable sightseeing experiences, each with its own distinct character and startling beauty.

Beyond the famous hells, Beppu offers world-class bathing in every conceivable style — from simple public bathhouses to luxurious ryokan, sand baths on the beach, and even mud baths. The city’s ancient tradition of jigoku mushi (hell-steaming) uses natural geothermal heat to cook food, a practice that dates back to the Edo period and continues to this day.

Attractions

From boiling hells of vivid color to panoramic mountain summits, Beppu’s attractions are as dramatic as the volcanic forces that created them.

Beppu Jigoku (Hells of Beppu)

別府地獄

The seven “hells” of Beppu are natural hot springs reaching temperatures of approximately 100°C — far too hot for bathing, these are spectacles for viewing only. For over 1,000 years, this area was avoided as “cursed” land. Today, the hells are protected as designated Places of Scenic Beauty and rank among Japan’s most extraordinary natural attractions.

Scenic Beauty 1,000+ Years
Umi Jigoku

Sea Hell — the most visually stunning, featuring a vast cobalt-blue pool heated to 98°C and reaching 200 meters deep. The vivid blue color comes from dissolved iron sulfate. The grounds also feature tropical Amazon water lilies that thrive in the naturally heated water.

Chinoike Jigoku

Blood Pond Hell — the oldest of the seven hells, known for its striking blood-red water caused by natural iron oxide and clay deposits. The vivid crimson surface steaming against the surrounding green landscape is one of Beppu’s most iconic and unsettling sights.

Other Hells

Tatsumaki Jigoku features a geyser that erupts every 30–40 minutes. Oniyama Jigoku (Devil Mountain) has raised over 80 crocodiles in naturally heated water for more than 100 years. Kamado Jigoku (Cooking Pot) is the most interactive, with six themed mini-hells including steam vents visitors can experience up close.

Food

The ancient tradition of jigoku mushi (hell-steaming) uses natural geothermal heat to cook food, a practice dating to the Edo period. Try the famous hell-boiled eggs (with their distinctive sulfuric taste) and the beloved Umi Jigoku pudding — a smooth custard steamed by volcanic heat.

Hours

Open daily 8:00–17:00. A combined ticket for all seven hells costs approximately ¥2,400. Allow 3–4 hours to visit all seven at a comfortable pace.

Mount Tsurumi (Beppu Ropeway)

鶴見岳

Rising 1,375 meters above Beppu, Mount Tsurumi offers one of the most spectacular panoramic views in all of Kyushu. A 10-minute ropeway ride carries visitors from the base to near the summit, where sweeping vistas unfold across Beppu city, the sparkling bay, and the surrounding volcanic mountain ranges.

1,375m Summit Panoramic Views
Views

From the summit viewpoint, the entire city of Beppu spreads out below with its distinctive columns of steam rising from hundreds of hot spring vents. On clear days, visitors can see across the Inland Sea all the way to the island of Shikoku on the opposite shore.

Trails

Short walking trails at the summit wind through alpine meadows and past small shrines. The mountain is particularly beautiful during autumn foliage season and in winter when frost crystals form on the vegetation at higher elevations.

Access

The Beppu Ropeway departs from a base station approximately 20 minutes by bus from central Beppu. The ride takes about 10 minutes each way. Dress warmly — temperatures at the summit can be significantly cooler than in the city below.

Visitor Tip

Start your day early at the Jigoku hells (they open at 8:00 AM) to beat the tour groups. Purchase the combined seven-hell ticket for the best value. After the hells, take the Beppu Ropeway up Mount Tsurumi for afternoon views, then end your day with a long soak in one of Beppu’s many public onsen. The hells are for viewing only — but the city has thousands of places where you can actually get in the water.
