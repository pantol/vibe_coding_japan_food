The World’s Greatest Metropolis

Tokyo — Where Past Meets Future

Tokyo is the world’s most populous metropolitan area — a staggering urban expanse where ultramodern skyscrapers, neon-drenched entertainment districts, and centuries-old temples coexist in a seamless blend of old and new. Each neighborhood is its own world: the electric energy of Shibuya, the otaku paradise of Akihabara, the historic charm of Asakusa, and the towering ambition of Shinjuku.

From Michelin-starred restaurants to standing ramen counters, from ancient shrines to cutting-edge technology showrooms, Tokyo offers an inexhaustible depth of experience that rewards repeat visits. It is a city that defies simple description — a place where tradition and innovation are not opposites but partners in an ever-evolving urban masterpiece.

Tokyo’s iconic neighborhoods — each a city within a city, each an experience unto itself.

Shinjuku

新宿

Home to the world’s busiest railway junction, processing approximately 3.5 million passengers daily. The west side bristles with skyscrapers, including the Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building with its free observatory on the 45th floor. The east side is a labyrinth of restaurants, bars, and shops that never seems to end.

Entertainment 3.5M Daily
Kabukicho

Japan’s largest entertainment district, a neon-soaked maze of restaurants, bars, clubs, and cinemas that pulses with energy well into the early hours.

Golden Gai

A labyrinth of roughly 200 tiny bars, most seating only 5–10 people each. This atmospheric collection of ramshackle buildings is a beloved relic of postwar Tokyo and a favorite haunt of artists, writers, and adventurous visitors.

Shinjuku Gyoen

One of Tokyo’s finest parks and a premier hanami (cherry blossom viewing) destination, combining Japanese, English, and French garden styles in a sprawling urban oasis.

Tip

Visit the Metropolitan Government Building observatory for free panoramic views. On clear days, you can see Mount Fuji. Open until 23:00.

Asakusa

浅草

The historic heart of Tokyo, centered on Senso-ji — the city’s oldest temple, founded in 645 AD. Visitors pass through the iconic Kaminarimon gate with its massive red lantern, then walk the 200-meter Nakamise-dori market street lined with traditional shops selling crafts, snacks, and souvenirs.

Historic Founded 645 AD
Senso-ji

Tokyo’s oldest temple, founded in 645 AD after two fishermen found a golden statue of Kannon (goddess of mercy) in the Sumida River. The temple complex includes the five-story pagoda and the main hall with its dramatic incense cauldron.

Atmosphere

Asakusa preserves the old-town “shitamachi” atmosphere of traditional Tokyo, with narrow streets, old-fashioned shops, and a pace of life that feels distinctly different from the modern metropolis surrounding it.

Skytree

From Asakusa, the Tokyo Skytree (634m) dominates the skyline. It is the world’s tallest tower and offers observation decks at 350m and 450m with staggering views of the city.

Tip

Visit early morning to experience the temple grounds before the crowds arrive. The Nakamise shops typically open around 9:00–10:00.

Akihabara

秋葉原

The world capital of otaku culture. Born as a post-WWII black market for radio parts, it evolved into an electronics mecca and then into the global epicenter of anime, manga, and gaming culture. Multi-story buildings like Mandarake, Radio Kaikan, and Super Potato overflow with collectibles, retro games, and rare finds.

Otaku Culture Electronics & Anime
Shops

Mandarake is the ultimate destination for manga and anime collectibles. Radio Kaikan houses floors of figure shops, trading cards, and hobby stores. Super Potato is a retro gaming paradise with playable classic consoles.

Culture

Maid cafes, cosplay shops, and themed restaurants abound. Akihabara embraces every facet of otaku subculture with an enthusiasm and sincerity that is unique in the world.

Sundays

On Sundays, the main Chuo-dori street becomes a pedestrian-only “Hokosha Tengoku” (Pedestrian Paradise), allowing visitors to stroll freely among the neon-lit storefronts.

Tip

Visit on a Sunday for the car-free Chuo-dori experience. Even non-otaku visitors will find Akihabara’s sensory overload fascinating.

Shibuya

渋谷

The icon of modern Tokyo. Shibuya Crossing — often called the “world’s busiest pedestrian crossing” — sees up to 3,000 people surging across from all directions with every light change. Outside the station stands the statue of Hachiko, the loyal Akita who waited nine years for his deceased owner at this spot from 1925 to 1935.

Modern Tokyo Iconic Crossing
Crossing

Shibuya’s scramble crossing is a mesmerizing spectacle of organized chaos. Up to 3,000 people cross simultaneously from all directions when the lights change, yet collisions are remarkably rare. Best viewed from above at the Shibuya Sky observation deck or the Starbucks overlooking the intersection.

Hachiko

The bronze statue of Hachiko commemorates Japan’s most famous dog — an Akita who faithfully returned to Shibuya Station every day for nine years after his owner’s death in 1925, waiting until his own passing in 1935. The statue is Tokyo’s most popular meeting point.

Shibuya Sky

The rooftop observation deck at Shibuya Scramble Square rises 229 meters, offering a stunning open-air panorama of the Tokyo skyline, the crossing below, and on clear days, Mount Fuji.

Tip

Visit the crossing at night for the full neon spectacle. Shibuya Sky is best at sunset for golden-hour views transitioning into the illuminated cityscape.

Visitor Tip

Tokyo’s neighborhoods are best experienced one at a time. Dedicate a morning to Asakusa and Senso-ji, then take the metro to Akihabara for an afternoon of sensory overload. Save Shinjuku and Shibuya for the evening when the neon reaches full intensity. A Suica or Pasmo IC card is essential for navigating the metro system — trains run with legendary punctuality until around midnight.
