Kobe Attractions

Port City of Wagyu, Sake & Hot Springs

Kobe is a cosmopolitan port city that was opened to foreign trade in 1868, creating a unique blend of Japanese and Western cultures that persists to this day. Nestled between the Rokko Mountains and Osaka Bay, the city offers an extraordinary combination of natural beauty, historic shrines, vibrant neighborhoods, and world-famous cuisine. From cascading waterfalls just minutes from the train station to one of Japan’s three great Chinatowns, Kobe rewards visitors with surprises at every turn.

Nunobiki Falls (Nunobiki-no-taki)

布引の滝

A series of four waterfalls just a 20-minute walk from Shin-Kobe station. The main waterfall Ontaki stands 43 meters tall and is designated one of Japan’s Top 100 Waterfalls. Mentioned by the court poetess Sei Shōnagon in her famous ‘Pillow Book.’ A ropeway leads further up to the Nunobiki Herb Gardens.

Nature Free
Duration

~2–2.5 hours leisurely walk round-trip from Shin-Kobe station.

Height

Main Ontaki waterfall is 43 meters tall.

History

Mentioned by Sei Shōnagon in ‘The Pillow Book’ — one of Japan’s most celebrated literary works.

Tip

Take the ropeway up to the herb gardens for panoramic views of the city and bay.

Sorakuen Garden

相楽園

One of Kobe’s most beautiful gardens, established in 1885 by a former daimyō. The garden artfully blends Japanese and Western styles with a central pond and traditional tea house. The historic Hassam House on the grounds survived the devastating 1995 Great Hanshin Earthquake. A peaceful retreat in the heart of the city.

Garden 9:00–17:00
Founded

1885, by a former daimyō.

Style

Japanese-Western mix with a central pond and traditional tea house.

Landmark

Hassam House — one of few structures that survived the 1995 earthquake.

Tip

A peaceful city retreat ideal for a quiet afternoon stroll.

Ikuta-jinja

生田神社

One of the oldest Shinto shrines in Japan, founded in 201 AD. Dedicated to the goddess Wakahirume, the younger sister of sun goddess Amaterasu. The city name “Kobe” itself derives from “Kanbe” — the people who served as this shrine’s servants. Repeatedly rebuilt after earthquakes and war, it stands as an enduring symbol of Kobe’s resilience.

Shrine Free, 7:00–17:00
Founded

201 AD — one of the oldest shrines in Japan.

Deity

Wakahirume, the younger sister of sun goddess Amaterasu.

Name Origin

“Kobe” derives from “Kanbe” — people who served as this shrine’s servants.

Significance

Symbol of Kobe’s resilience — rebuilt repeatedly after destruction.

Kobe Chinatown (Nankin-machi)

南京町

One of Japan’s three great Chinatowns (alongside Yokohama and Nagasaki). Small but vibrant, with roughly 100 shops and restaurants packed into just 200 meters of bustling streets. The essential street food trio: nikuman (steamed meat buns), xiaolongbao (soup dumplings), and sesame balls.

Neighborhood Open Daily
Scale

~100 shops and restaurants in just 200 meters.

Must-Try

Nikuman (steamed meat buns), xiaolongbao, and sesame balls.

Status

One of Japan’s three great Chinatowns.

Tip

Visit at lunchtime for maximum energy; combine with Motomachi shopping street.

Kobe Beef

神戸ビーフ

The legendary wagyu from Tajima-ushi cattle raised in Hyogo Prefecture. Famous for its intense marbling known as shimofuri, which creates an extraordinary buttery texture. Only certified restaurants display the official Kobe Beef logo and certificate number. Expect to pay from ~5,000 to 15,000 yen and above for an authentic steak experience.

Culinary Icon ¥5,000–15,000+
Origin

Tajima-ushi cattle raised exclusively in Hyogo Prefecture.

Quality

Intense marbling (shimofuri) that melts at low temperature, creating legendary buttery sweetness.

Certification

Certified restaurants display the official Kobe Beef logo and certificate number.

Price

Steak from ~¥5,000 to ¥15,000+ depending on cut and grade.

Visitor Tip

Kobe is remarkably compact and walkable. Nunobiki Falls, Ikuta Shrine, Nankin-machi, and the teppanyaki restaurants of Sannomiya are all within a few kilometers of each other. Start with the waterfalls in the morning, visit the shrine, then work your way through Chinatown for lunch before an evening steak dinner. The Nada sake breweries are a short train ride east for afternoon tastings.
