Yakushima

屋久島

The Ancient Cedar Island

Overview Attractions
Attraction Guide

The Primeval Island

Yakushima is a roughly circular, mountainous island off the southern tip of Kyushu in Kagoshima Prefecture — a UNESCO World Heritage Site where approximately 90% of the land is covered in dense, ancient forest. Recognized in 1993 for the outstanding universal value of its temperate rainforest ecosystem, the island is one of the most extraordinary natural destinations in all of Japan.

The island is home to the legendary yakusugi — ancient Japanese cedar trees (Cryptomeria japonica) over 1,000 years old. The oldest and most famous of these, the Jōmon Sugi, has an estimated age of 2,200–7,200 years, making it one of the oldest known living trees on earth. These towering, gnarled giants grow in a landscape of moss-draped ravines, cascading waterfalls, and mist-shrouded peaks.

Yakushima is famously known as the inspiration for the enchanted forest in Hayao Miyazaki’s Studio Ghibli masterpiece Princess Mononoke (1997). The island’s otherworldly atmosphere — where ancient trees emerge from carpets of luminous green moss and clouds drift through the canopy — makes it a destination unlike any other.

Yakusugi Museum (Yakusugi Shizenkan)

屋久杉自然館

An excellent introduction to Yakushima’s ecology and history. The museum traces the island’s Edo-era logging history and explains the difference between regular sugi (Japanese cedar) and yakusugi — trees over 1,000 years old whose exceptionally high resin content makes the wood remarkably rot-resistant.

Museum & Education Visit Before Trekking
Why Visit First

The museum provides essential context for understanding the ancient forests you’ll trek through. Learning about the ecology, the logging history, and the significance of the yakusugi before seeing them in person transforms the hiking experience. Visit BEFORE trekking to Jōmon Sugi for the fullest appreciation.

Trailhead Access

The bus to the Jōmon Sugi trail (Arakawa trailhead) departs from directly in front of the museum. During the March–November restricted access period, this is the mandatory departure point for hikers, making it a natural first stop before heading into the forest.

Shiratani Unsuikyō

白谷雲水峡

A ravine and primeval forest park that inspired the enchanted forest in Studio Ghibli’s Princess Mononoke (1997). Lead background artist Oga Kazuo spent hours sketching the moss-covered trees and stones of this forest. Home to over 600 species of moss, making it one of the richest bryophyte habitats on earth.

Primeval Forest Donation Entry
Trail Routes

Three routes of varying length and difficulty: the Yayoi-sugi course (approximately 1 hour), the Bugyosugi course (approximately 3 hours), and the Taikoiwa Round Trip (4–5 hours). The trail to Kokemusu-no-mori (Moss Forest) — the actual “Mononoke spot” that inspired the film — leads through a fairytale landscape of luminous green moss draped over ancient trees and boulders.

Taikoiwa

The longest trail leads to Taikoiwa rock, a massive granite outcrop offering panoramic views over the forest canopy and out to the valley below. The climb is rewarding but demanding — allow 4–5 hours for the full round trip.

Wildlife

Yakushima has 90% forest cover and UNESCO protection since 1993. Yakushika deer and yakuzaru macaques are commonly spotted along the trails, often grazing calmly just meters from hikers.

Visitor Tip

Located approximately 30 minutes by car from Miyanoura port. Entry is by voluntary donation (conservation contribution). Bring rain gear — Yakushima receives extraordinary amounts of rainfall, and the forest is at its most magical when mist drifts through the canopy.

Jōmon Sugi

縄文杉

The oldest and thickest known Cryptomeria japonica tree in the world. With a trunk circumference of 16.4 meters, the Jōmon Sugi has an estimated age of 2,200–7,200 years. Rediscovered in 1966, the find led to the end of logging in the Arakawa region by 1970. The tree is viewed from a platform approximately 15 meters away to protect its root system.

Ancient Cedar ~22km / 9–10 hours
The Trail

The 22 km round trip from the Arakawa trailhead requires 9–10 hours of hiking. The first 9 km follow old forestry logging railway tracks — relatively flat and easy walking. After that, the Okabu trail turns to steep wooden stairs, root networks, and increasingly demanding terrain as you climb deeper into the ancient forest.

Notable Trees

The trail passes several remarkable trees: Sandai-sugi (38.4 meters tall), Wilson Stump (a huge stump you can enter inside — look up through the canopy to see the famous heart-shaped hole!), Meoto-sugi (the “married couple” trees joined together by a branch), and Daio-sugi (the “King of Cedars”). Jōmon Sugi itself is viewed from a platform approximately 15 meters away.

Access

From March through November, private cars cannot reach the Arakawa trailhead. You must take the designated bus from Yakusugi Museum. START your hike between 5:00 and 7:00 AM to complete the trail in daylight. This is a serious full-day hike — proper preparation is essential.

Visitor Tip

Bring: packed food and water (no shops on the trail), rain gear (essential — Yakushima is one of the wettest places in Japan), a headlamp or flashlight, and sturdy hiking boots. Start early (5:00–7:00 AM). Bus departs from Yakusugi Museum. Toilets are available at the trailhead and at several points along the route.

Yakusugi Land

屋久杉ランド

A primeval forest park offering multiple trails ranging from 30 minutes to 2.5 hours, significantly less demanding than the Jōmon Sugi trek. Wooden boardwalks provide accessible routes through stands of ancient yakusugi cedars, making it an excellent option for visitors of all fitness levels or a good backup if weather limits longer hikes.

Forest Park 30 min – 2.5 hours
Trails

The most popular trail takes approximately 2 hours and winds through ancient forest on well-maintained wooden boardwalks. Shorter 30-minute loops are available for those with limited time. The park provides direct access to old yakusugi trees without the commitment of a full-day expedition.

When to Visit

Yakusugi Land is an excellent complement to the main trails, especially if weather conditions limit longer treks. The boardwalks make it accessible even in wet conditions, and the forest atmosphere is just as enchanting as on the more demanding routes.

Visitor Tips — Yakushima

Yakushima receives some of the heaviest rainfall in Japan — rain gear is essential for every outing. For the Jōmon Sugi trek, start between 5:00–7:00 AM and bring food, water, headlamp, and good boots. The bus from Yakusugi Museum is mandatory March–November. Spring and autumn offer the most comfortable conditions. The island is reached by ferry or flight from Kagoshima.
