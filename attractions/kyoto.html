Kyoto

京都

Ancient Capital of a Thousand Temples

Overview Attractions
Ancient Capital

A Thousand Years of Imperial Splendor

Kyoto served as Japan’s imperial capital for over a millennium, from 794 until the Meiji Restoration in 1868. During this extraordinary span, the city became the cradle of Japanese high culture — the birthplace of the tea ceremony, ikebana, Noh theater, and the refined aesthetic sensibility known as miyabi. Today, Kyoto remains the spiritual heart of Japan, home to more than 2,000 temples, shrines, and gardens that embody centuries of artistic and philosophical achievement.

With 17 UNESCO World Heritage Sites, Kyoto offers an unparalleled concentration of cultural treasures. From the shimmering gold leaf of Kinkaku-ji to the mesmerizing tunnel of vermillion torii gates at Fushimi Inari, from the serene Zen rock gardens of Ryōan-ji to the ancient bamboo groves of Arashiyama, the city presents a living tapestry of Japanese history and aesthetics. Each season transforms the city — cherry blossoms paint it pink in spring, while fiery maples set the hillsides ablaze in autumn.

Beyond the famous landmarks, Kyoto’s charm lies in its atmospheric neighborhoods: the geisha district of Gion with its wooden machiya townhouses, the covered Nishiki Market bustling with centuries of culinary tradition, and the quiet cobblestone lanes of Higashiyama where time seems to stand still. Whether you spend three days or three weeks, Kyoto reveals new layers of beauty at every turn.

Nijo Castle (Nijo-jo)

二条城

Built in 1603 by Shogun Tokugawa Ieyasu as a symbol of his power, Nijo Castle is a UNESCO World Heritage Site renowned for its elaborate architecture, exquisite Kano school paintings, and the famous “nightingale floors” (uguisubari) that chirp underfoot to warn of intruders.

Castle & Palace UNESCO
History

Constructed in 1603 as the Kyoto residence of Tokugawa Ieyasu, the first Tokugawa shogun. It was here that the last Tokugawa shogun announced the restoration of imperial rule in 1867.

Highlights

The Ninomaru Palace features stunning sliding door paintings by artists of the Kano school. The nightingale floors were ingeniously designed to squeak when walked upon, serving as a security measure against intruders.

Tips

Hours: 8:45–17:00 (last entry 16:00). Closed some Tuesdays in Jan, Jul, Aug, Dec. The castle gardens are beautiful during cherry blossom season with evening illuminations.

Tofuku-ji

東福寺

One of the five great Zen temples of Kyoto, founded in 1236. Tofuku-ji is celebrated for the breathtaking autumn maple views from the Tsutenkyo Bridge and for the striking modernist Hojo zen garden designed by Shigemori Mirei in 1939.

Zen Temple Partially Free
History

Founded in 1236 by the statesman Kujo Michiie, who envisioned a temple to rival Nara’s great Todai-ji and Kofuku-ji — hence the name Tofuku-ji, combining characters from both.

Highlights

The Tsutenkyo (Bridge to Heaven) offers one of Kyoto’s most spectacular autumn foliage views. The Hojo garden by Shigemori Mirei (1939) is a masterpiece of modern Zen garden design featuring moss-covered checkerboard patterns.

Tips

The main grounds are free to enter; the Tsutenkyo Bridge and Hojo garden require separate admission fees. Peak autumn colors occur in mid-to-late November — arrive early to avoid enormous crowds.

Saihoji (Kokedera / Moss Temple)

西芳寺

A UNESCO World Heritage Site famed for its extraordinary carpet of over 120 species of moss covering the temple grounds. Originally founded in the Nara era, the temple was redesigned by the legendary Zen master Muso Soseki in 1339 into the ethereal moss garden seen today.

Zen Temple UNESCO · Reservation Required
History

Originally founded during the Nara era, the temple was transformed in 1339 by Zen master Muso Soseki. The moss garden was not planned — it developed naturally over centuries, creating one of Japan’s most otherworldly landscapes.

Highlights

Over 120 species of moss blanket the garden in infinite shades of green. The visit begins with a sutra copying ceremony (shakyo) inside the temple before guests are permitted to walk through the garden.

Tips

Advance reservation is required — book online through the official website. Visits include a sutra copying session. The limited access helps preserve the garden’s delicate ecosystem and ensures a serene experience.

Kiyomizu-dera

清水寺

Founded in 778, this iconic temple features a massive wooden stage that juts out from the hillside, supported by 139 pillars standing 13 meters high — all constructed without a single nail. The Otowa waterfall below offers three streams, each said to grant a different blessing.

Buddhist Temple UNESCO
History

Founded in 778, predating the establishment of Kyoto as capital. The current main hall dates from 1633, rebuilt by Tokugawa Iemitsu. The name means “Pure Water Temple,” referring to the Otowa waterfall.

Highlights

The wooden veranda offers panoramic views of Kyoto. The Otowa waterfall’s three streams are said to grant longevity, success in studies, and a fortunate love life — but drinking from all three is considered greedy.

Tips

Open 6:00–18:00 (extended hours during special illuminations). Visit at opening time to experience the temple in relative peace. The approach through Higashiyama’s traditional streets is part of the experience.

Sannenzaka & Ninenzaka

三年坂・二年坂

Historic cobblestone streets leading up to Kiyomizu-dera, lined with traditional wooden machiya houses, souvenir shops, and tea houses. Local legend warns that stumbling on these slopes brings bad luck — two years of misfortune on Ninenzaka, three on Sannenzaka.

Historic District Free
History

These stone-paved slopes have served as the pilgrimage approach to Kiyomizu-dera for centuries. The names literally mean “Three-Year Slope” and “Two-Year Slope,” referring to the years of misfortune said to befall anyone who trips.

Highlights

Traditional shops selling Kyoto ceramics (Kiyomizu-yaki), sweets, and crafts. Home to the world’s only Starbucks housed inside a restored two-story machiya townhouse, blending seamlessly into the historic streetscape.

Tips

Visit early morning or late afternoon to avoid peak crowds. The streets are beautifully atmospheric at dusk when lanterns begin to glow. Wear comfortable shoes — the stone steps can be slippery when wet.

Gion

祇園

Kyoto’s most famous geisha (geiko) district, where traditional wooden ochaya (tea houses) line the atmospheric Hanamikoji street. Strict architectural regulations preserve the district’s Edo-period character, and you may glimpse a geiko or maiko hurrying to an evening engagement.

Historic District Free
History

Gion evolved from a neighborhood serving pilgrims to Yasaka Shrine into Kyoto’s premier entertainment and geisha district. Its ochaya (tea houses) have hosted exclusive gatherings for centuries.

Highlights

Hanamikoji street is the district’s main thoroughfare, lined with exclusive ochaya. Gion Corner offers nightly shows featuring tea ceremony, flower arranging, court music, and traditional dance for visitors.

Tips

Be respectful — do not chase, block, or photograph geiko and maiko without permission. Evening is the best time to visit when the lanterns are lit. Strict regulations protect the district’s historic architecture.

Kodai-ji

高台寺

A beautiful Zen temple established in 1606 by Nene, the widow of the great warlord Toyotomi Hideyoshi, to pray for his soul. Kodai-ji is renowned for its exquisite maki-e lacquerware, gardens designed by the master Kobori Enshu, and spectacular seasonal night illuminations.

Zen Temple ¥600
History

Built in 1606 by Kita-no-Mandokoro (Nene), the principal wife of Toyotomi Hideyoshi, as a memorial temple after his death in 1598. The temple received support from Tokugawa Ieyasu.

Highlights

The temple houses exceptional maki-e lacquerware decorated with designs by masters of the age. The gardens, attributed to Kobori Enshu, feature beautiful dry landscape compositions and reflective ponds.

Tips

The seasonal night illuminations (spring and autumn) are among Kyoto’s most popular, featuring dramatic projection mapping displays on the gardens. Arrive early for evening events to secure a good viewing spot.

Yasaka-jinja

八坂神社

A venerable 7th-century Shinto shrine at the heart of Gion, famous as the home of the magnificent Gion Matsuri festival held each July. The adjacent Maruyama Park is Kyoto’s most beloved cherry blossom viewing spot, centered around a magnificent weeping cherry tree.

Shinto Shrine Free · Open 24/7
History

Established in the 7th century, Yasaka Shrine has been the guardian shrine of Gion for over 1,300 years. It is the starting point of the Gion Matsuri, one of Japan’s three greatest festivals.

Highlights

The Gion Matsuri (the entire month of July, with grand processions on July 17 and 24) is one of the most spectacular festivals in the world. Maruyama Park’s iconic weeping cherry tree is illuminated at night during spring.

Tips

Free admission, open 24 hours. The shrine is beautifully lit at night and serves as a natural gateway between Gion and Maruyama Park. Food stalls line the approach during festivals and New Year.

Fushimi Inari-Taisha

伏見稲荷大社

The most important of Japan’s 30,000 Inari shrines, spectacularly famous for its thousands of vermillion torii gates that form a mesmerizing tunnel up the sacred Mount Inari. The full 4-kilometer trail to the summit takes 2–3 hours and is one of Kyoto’s most unforgettable experiences.

Shinto Shrine Free · Open 24/7
History

Founded in 711 AD, Fushimi Inari is the head shrine of Inari, the Shinto god of rice, prosperity, and business. The torii gates are donated by individuals and businesses seeking good fortune.

Highlights

The iconic Senbon Torii (thousands of gates) creates a surreal tunnel of vermillion that snakes up the mountainside. Sub-shrines, fox statues, and viewpoints dot the trail. Each gate bears the donor’s name and date of dedication.

Tips

Free admission, open 24 hours. Visit at dawn or after dark for a mystical, crowd-free experience. The full loop trail to the 233-meter summit takes 2–3 hours. Small restaurants at the Yotsutsuji intersection offer refreshments and panoramic city views.

Arashiyama, Gioji & Tenryu-ji

嵐山

The enchanting western district of Kyoto, anchored by the towering bamboo grove with stalks reaching 30 meters high, the elegant Togetsukyo bridge, and Tenryu-ji — a 1339 UNESCO World Heritage temple with the stunning Sogenchi pond garden. Nearby Gioji is a hidden moss-covered retreat.

Scenic Area Tenryu-ji: UNESCO
History

Arashiyama has been a favored retreat of Kyoto’s aristocracy since the Heian period. Tenryu-ji was founded in 1339 by Ashikaga Takauji and designed by Muso Soseki. Gioji is associated with a tragic tale from the Tales of the Heike.

Highlights

The bamboo grove is otherworldly — towering stalks creak and sway overhead. Tenryu-ji’s Sogenchi garden is one of Japan’s earliest examples of “borrowed scenery” (shakkei), incorporating the Arashiyama mountains into its composition. Gioji’s moss garden is serene and rarely crowded.

Tips

Visit the bamboo grove at sunrise to avoid crowds. Rent a bicycle to explore the wider area efficiently. The Togetsukyo bridge area is spectacular in autumn. Combine Tenryu-ji with a walk north to Gioji for a half-day excursion.

Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion)

金閣寺

One of Japan’s most iconic images — a three-story pavilion covered entirely in real gold leaf, reflected in the still waters of Kyoko-chi (Mirror Pond). Built in 1397 by Shogun Ashikaga Yoshimitsu, infamously burned down by a monk in 1950, and faithfully rebuilt in 1955 with even more gold than the original.

Zen Temple UNESCO
History

Originally built as a retirement villa for Shogun Ashikaga Yoshimitsu in 1397 and converted to a Zen temple after his death. Each of its three stories represents a different architectural style: shinden, samurai, and Zen. A golden phoenix crowns the roof.

Highlights

The pavilion’s gold leaf covering blazes in sunlight and creates a stunning reflection in the pond. The surrounding strolling garden is a masterpiece of Muromachi-period landscape design. The arson of 1950 inspired Yukio Mishima’s novel “The Temple of the Golden Pavilion.”

Tips

Open 9:00–17:00 year-round. The admission ticket is a beautiful calligraphic charm (ofuda). Best photographs are taken in the morning when light illuminates the gold. Visit on a windless day for perfect reflections.

Ginkaku-ji (Silver Pavilion)

銀閣寺

Despite its name, the Silver Pavilion was never actually covered in silver. Built in 1482 by Shogun Ashikaga Yoshimasa, it embodies the refined aesthetic of wabi-sabi — beauty found in imperfection and transience. The Ginshadan silver sand garden, raked into a cone and wave pattern, is mesmerizing.

Zen Temple UNESCO
History

Built in 1482 by Ashikaga Yoshimasa, grandson of Kinkaku-ji’s builder, as a retirement villa modeled after the Golden Pavilion. The planned silver foil covering was never applied, possibly due to the Ōnin War’s disruption.

Highlights

The Ginshadan (Silver Sand Sea) and Kogetsudai (Moon-Viewing Platform) sand garden are unique in all of Japan. The moss garden and hillside walking path offer lovely views over the city. A profound symbol of wabi-sabi aesthetics.

Tips

Open 8:30–17:00 (shorter winter hours). Located at the northern end of the Philosopher’s Path — start here and walk south toward Nanzen-ji for a wonderful half-day walk through eastern Kyoto.

Philosopher’s Path

哲学の道

A peaceful 2-kilometer stone path following a cherry-tree-lined canal from Ginkaku-ji southward to Nanzen-ji. Named after the influential Kyoto School philosopher Nishida Kitaro, who practiced daily walking meditation along this route, it is one of Kyoto’s most beloved strolls — especially during cherry blossom season.

Scenic Walk Free
History

Named after philosopher Nishida Kitaro (1870–1945), founder of the Kyoto School of philosophy, who used this path for daily contemplative walks. The cherry trees were planted in the early 20th century by artist Hashimoto Kansetsu.

Highlights

In spring, the canal is canopied by hundreds of cherry trees creating a tunnel of pink blossoms. Quaint cafes, boutique shops, and small temples dot the route. Autumn brings beautiful foliage reflections in the canal.

Tips

Free and open 24/7. The walk takes about 30–45 minutes without stops. Walk from north (Ginkaku-ji) to south to visit several small temples along the way. Early morning is most peaceful and atmospheric.

Nanzen-ji

南禅寺

The highest-ranking Zen temple in all of Japan, founded in 1291. Nanzen-ji is renowned for its enormous Sanmon gate offering panoramic views of the city, its serene sub-temples, and an unexpected Meiji-era Roman-style brick aqueduct that runs through the temple grounds.

Zen Temple Grounds Free
History

Founded in 1291 on the site of Emperor Kameyama’s retirement villa. It holds the highest rank among Japan’s Zen temples in the Gozan (Five Mountain) system. The massive Sanmon gate dates from 1628.

Highlights

Climb the Sanmon gate for sweeping views of Kyoto (featured in the kabuki play “Sanmon Gosan no Kiri”). The Suirokaku aqueduct, built in 1888 to channel water from Lake Biwa, is a photogenic blend of East and West. Sub-temple Tenju-an has beautiful gardens.

Tips

The grounds are free to explore. Sanmon gate, Hojo garden, and sub-temples have separate admission fees. Located at the southern end of the Philosopher’s Path. Combine with Eikando next door for a rich half-day.

Eikando (Zenrin-ji)

永観堂

Widely regarded as the single best spot in all of Kyoto for autumn leaves. Eikando’s hillside grounds become a blazing tapestry of red and gold each November. The temple also houses the unusual Mikaeri Amida — a Buddha statue that looks back over its shoulder, symbolizing compassion for those who fall behind.

Buddhist Temple Night Illuminations
History

Founded in 853, the temple became associated with the Jodo (Pure Land) sect in the 11th century. Its reputation as Kyoto’s premier autumn foliage destination has been celebrated in poetry for over a thousand years.

Highlights

The Mikaeri Amida statue depicts the Buddha glancing backward — a unique and moving image in Japanese Buddhism. During autumn night illuminations, the maple trees are lit up in dramatic color, reflected in the Hojo Pond.

Tips

Standard admission applies, with a higher fee during the autumn foliage season (mid-November). Evening illumination events have separate tickets and can involve long queues. Adjacent to Nanzen-ji — visit both together.

Heian-jingu

平安神宮

Built in 1895 to commemorate the 1,100th anniversary of Kyoto’s founding as Japan’s capital. The shrine is a two-thirds scale replica of the original Heian-era Imperial Palace, fronted by one of Japan’s largest torii gates standing an impressive 24 meters tall. The Shin-en strolling gardens are among Kyoto’s finest.

Shinto Shrine Garden: ¥600
History

Constructed in 1895 for the 1,100th anniversary of the transfer of the capital to Kyoto (Heian-kyo) in 794. It enshrines Emperor Kanmu, who established the capital, and Emperor Komei, the last emperor to reside in Kyoto.

Highlights

The massive vermillion torii gate (24 meters) is one of Japan’s largest. The Shin-en gardens wrap around the shrine in four sections, featuring a stunning weeping cherry collection, stepping stones across a pond, and a covered bridge.

Tips

The shrine grounds are free; the Shin-en gardens require ¥600 admission and are highly recommended, especially during cherry blossom season when the weeping cherries create a pink canopy. The Jidai Matsuri festival starts here each October 22.

Kamigamo-jinja

上賀茂神社

One of Kyoto’s oldest Shinto shrines, dating to the 6th century and designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The shrine is distinguished by its iconic pair of tatesuna — conical sand mounds at the entrance that represent sacred mountains and are believed to purify the approach.

Shinto Shrine UNESCO · Free
History

One of the oldest shrines in Japan, with origins in the 6th century. It is dedicated to Kamo Wake-ikazuchi, the thunder deity. The shrine has been rebuilt regularly in the traditional style for over 1,400 years.

Highlights

The tatesuna (standing sand cones) at the entrance are striking symbols of purification. The shrine is the starting point of the Aoi Matsuri (Hollyhock Festival) in May, one of Kyoto’s three great festivals, dating back to the 6th century.

Tips

Free admission to the grounds. The shrine is located in northern Kyoto, away from the main tourist clusters, making it a peaceful escape. Visit during the Aoi Matsuri (May 15) to see the magnificent procession in Heian-period costumes.

Kyoto Gyoen (Imperial Palace Park)

京都御苑

A vast public park surrounding the Kyoto Imperial Palace, spanning an impressive 1,300 by 700 meters in the heart of the city. This green oasis offers expansive lawns, gravel paths, and over 50,000 trees, making it one of Kyoto’s premier spots for cherry blossoms in spring and plum blossoms in late winter.

Park & Palace Free
History

The Kyoto Imperial Palace served as the residence of Japan’s emperors from 1331 until the capital moved to Tokyo in 1869. The surrounding park was created in 1869 when the noble residences around the palace were cleared.

Highlights

Early-blooming weeping cherries (shidarezakura) and late-blooming Gosho-zakura make the park one of Kyoto’s best cherry blossom destinations. The plum grove blooms in February–March. The palace itself can be visited freely without reservation.

Tips

Free admission to both the park and palace (guided tours no longer required). Open dawn to dusk. A wonderful spot for a quiet morning walk or picnic. Several gates provide access from surrounding streets. Easily combined with Shimogamo Shrine nearby.

Nishiki Market

錦市場

Known as the “Kitchen of Kyoto,” this 400-meter covered market has been the city’s premier food destination for over 400 years. More than 100 shops and stalls offer an extraordinary array of Kyoto specialties including tsukemono pickles, fresh tofu, wagashi sweets, and seasonal ingredients.

Market Free Entry
History

The market traces its origins to the early 1600s when fresh fish merchants began gathering in this location. Over four centuries, it has grown into a 400-meter arcade housing over 100 specialized food vendors.

Highlights

Sample Kyoto’s famous tsukemono (pickled vegetables), silky fresh tofu, yuba (tofu skin), artisan wagashi sweets, and seasonal produce. Many vendors offer small tasting portions, making it ideal for a culinary walking tour.

Tips

Most shops open around 9:00–10:00 and close by 17:00–18:00. Visit mid-morning for the best experience before crowds peak. Some vendors close on Wednesdays or Sundays. Eat while walking is increasingly discouraged — consume purchases at the vendor’s standing area.

Sanjusangendo

三十三間堂

Home to one of Japan’s most astonishing sights: 1,001 gilded statues of Kannon, the goddess of mercy, standing in rows inside the longest wooden building in Japan at 120 meters. Originally built in 1164 and flanked by 28 guardian deities, the effect is overwhelming and unforgettable.

Buddhist Temple ¥600
History

Built in 1164 by Taira no Kiyomori for Emperor Go-Shirakawa. The name refers to the 33 (sanjusan) bays between the building’s pillars. The current hall dates from 1266 after the original burned. The Toshiya archery contest has been held here since the Edo period.

Highlights

The 1,001 statues of the Thousand-Armed Kannon are arranged in 10 rows of 50 on each side of a large central seated Kannon. Each statue is slightly different. The 28 guardian deities include figures from Hindu mythology. Legend says that among the thousand faces, you will find one that resembles someone you have lost.

Tips

Open 8:00–17:00 (shorter winter hours). Photography is not permitted inside. Take time to walk slowly along the rows — the scale and detail become more impressive the longer you look. The annual Toshiya archery event is held in January.

Komyo-in

光明院

A hidden gem and sub-temple of Tofuku-ji, Komyo-in features an exquisite Zen garden designed by the modernist master Shigemori Mirei in 1939. Far less crowded than the main temple, it offers a tranquil space for quiet contemplation of one of Kyoto’s most artistically daring garden compositions.

Zen Temple Donation
History

Founded as a sub-temple of Tofuku-ji in 1391. The garden, called Hashin-tei (Wave Heart Garden), was created by Shigemori Mirei in 1939 and represents his bold, modernist approach to traditional Zen garden design.

Highlights

The garden features dynamic arrangements of standing stones, raked gravel, and carefully placed moss and azalea bushes. Unlike many famous gardens, you can often enjoy it in complete solitude, sitting on the veranda in meditative peace.

Tips

Admission is by voluntary donation (place it in the box at the entrance). Located a short walk south of Tofuku-ji station. Combine with a visit to the main Tofuku-ji complex. One of Kyoto’s best-kept secrets for garden lovers.

Shirakawa Canal

白川

One of Kyoto’s most photogenic waterways, the Shirakawa Canal winds through the Gion district lined with graceful willow trees, cherry blossoms, and traditional tea houses. In the evening, soft lantern light reflects off the water, creating an atmosphere of timeless beauty that epitomizes old Kyoto.

Scenic Waterway Free
History

The Shirakawa (White River) has flowed through eastern Kyoto for centuries, drawing its crystal-clear water from the mountains above. The canal became central to Gion’s atmosphere as tea houses and restaurants were built along its banks.

Highlights

The stretch near Shinbashi-dori is particularly scenic, with weeping willows, stone bridges, and traditional machiya facades. Cherry blossoms overhang the water in spring. Evening lanterns from the tea houses create magical reflections.

Tips

Free and accessible 24/7. The most atmospheric time to visit is early evening when the lanterns are lit and the streets are quieter. The Shinbashi area is a Preservation District for Groups of Traditional Buildings. Combine with an evening walk through Gion.

Visitor Tip

Kyoto’s temples and shrines generally open between 8:00–9:00 and close by 17:00. Many major sites offer special evening illuminations during cherry blossom season (late March–mid April) and autumn foliage season (mid-November–early December). Purchase a one-day bus pass (¥700) for unlimited rides on city buses. Wear shoes that slip on and off easily, as you’ll be removing them frequently at temple halls. Be mindful of photography restrictions inside temple buildings, and always follow posted etiquette guidelines in the geisha districts.
