Nagasaki — 400 Years of International Exchange

For more than 400 years, Nagasaki has been Japan’s gateway to the outside world. This historic port city bears the indelible marks of Chinese, Dutch, and Portuguese influences that have shaped its architecture, cuisine, and culture into something uniquely cosmopolitan within Japan.

During Japan’s two centuries of self-imposed isolation, Nagasaki’s tiny artificial island of Dejima served as the nation’s only window to Western civilization. Today, the city’s layered history — from hidden Christian communities to atomic devastation to vibrant international heritage — makes it one of the most fascinating and emotionally resonant destinations in all of Japan.

Nagasaki’s landmarks span centuries of international exchange, wartime tragedy, and enduring resilience.

Peace Park & Museum

平和公園

On August 9, 1945, the plutonium bomb “Fat Man” was dropped on Nagasaki, killing approximately 40,000 people instantly — a figure that rose to roughly 70,000 by the end of the year. The park’s centerpiece is the 10-meter Peace Statue by sculptor Kitamura Seibo: one hand points skyward, symbolizing the nuclear threat, while the other extends horizontally, representing peace.

Peace Memorial Free (Park)
Museum

The Nagasaki Atomic Bomb Museum chronicles the events leading up to and following the bombing through photographs, artifacts, and survivor testimonies. A deeply moving and essential experience.

Statue

Kitamura Seibo’s iconic Peace Statue: the raised right hand warns of the nuclear threat, while the extended left hand symbolizes eternal peace. The closed eyes represent a prayer for the victims.

Fountain

The Peace Fountain was built in memory of those who died of thirst after the bombing, desperately seeking water in the devastated city.

Tip

Museum open 8:30–17:30. The park itself is free and accessible at all times. Allow at least 1.5–2 hours for a thorough visit.

Glover Garden & Oura Cathedral

グラバー園

An open-air museum showcasing 19th-century Western residences on a hillside overlooking Nagasaki harbor. The centerpiece is the house of Thomas Glover, a Scottish merchant who played a pivotal role in helping modernize Japan. His love story with a Japanese woman named Tsuru may have inspired Puccini’s immortal opera “Madame Butterfly.”

Heritage Site UNESCO Connection
Glover

Thomas Blake Glover arrived in Nagasaki in 1859 and became instrumental in Japan’s modernization, helping to establish shipbuilding, coal mining, and the foundations of what would become Mitsubishi. His residence is the oldest Western-style building in Japan.

Cathedral

Oura Cathedral (1864) is the oldest surviving church in Japan and a designated National Treasure. It is part of the UNESCO-listed Hidden Christian Sites of the Nagasaki Region, commemorating the remarkable survival of Christian communities through 250 years of persecution.

Views

The garden’s hillside location provides sweeping panoramic views of Nagasaki’s harbor and the surrounding mountains.

Tip

Allow approximately 1 hour for the garden. Take the moving walkways up the hill and stroll back down at your leisure.

Dejima

出島

A fan-shaped artificial island built in 1636 that served as Japan’s sole window to the Western world for over 200 years. During the sakoku period of national isolation (1641–1854), only the Dutch East India Company was permitted to trade here. The Dutch traders were virtual prisoners, confined to this tiny island.

Historic Island 200+ Years of History
Significance

Western science, medicine, and technology entered Japan exclusively through Dejima during the isolation period. This flow of knowledge became known as rangaku (“Dutch studies”), and it kept Japan connected to global scientific advances even during its centuries of seclusion.

Today

The island has been meticulously reconstructed with period buildings, furnishings, and exhibits that vividly recreate the daily life of Dutch merchants and their interactions with Japanese officials and interpreters.

Layout

The distinctive fan shape was designed to make surveillance easier. The island measured just 120m by 75m — a remarkably small space through which an entire nation’s foreign contact was funneled.

Tip

Allow approximately 1 hour. The reconstructed buildings and exhibits bring this remarkable chapter of history to life.

Sofuku-ji, Megane Bridge, Dutch Slope

崇福寺

Three iconic landmarks that showcase Nagasaki’s multicultural heritage. Sofuku-ji is a Chinese Zen temple built in 1629 in authentic Ming Dynasty style — the only one of its kind in Japan. Its gate and main hall are designated National Treasures. Megane-bashi (Spectacles Bridge) is the oldest stone arch bridge in Japan, dating to 1634.

Cultural Heritage National Treasures
Sofuku-ji

Founded in 1629 by Chinese monk Chaonian for the Chinese community in Nagasaki. Built entirely in Ming Dynasty architectural style, it is the only temple of its kind in Japan. The Daiippomon gate and Daiyuhoden main hall are both National Treasures.

Megane-bashi

The oldest stone arch bridge in Japan (1634). Its double arches reflected in the Nakashima River create the shape of spectacles (megane), giving the bridge its charming name.

Dutch Slope

Oranda-zaka is a historic stone-paved street leading to the former Dutch residential enclave. The steep, atmospheric lane is lined with old Western-style buildings and offers a tangible connection to Nagasaki’s international past.

Tip

These three sites are relatively close together and can be visited in a single walking route through the historic center of Nagasaki.

Inasa-yama

稲佐山

Rising 333 meters above the city, Inasa-yama is celebrated as one of the “Three Best Night Views in Japan” alongside Hakodate and Kobe. A ropeway carries visitors to the summit, where the panoramic vista of illuminated Nagasaki, its harbor, and surrounding islands has been compared to “10 million dollars’ worth of diamonds.”

Night View Top 3 in Japan
Height

333 meters above sea level, offering unobstructed 360-degree views of the city, harbor, and islands.

Ranking

Consistently ranked among Japan’s three most spectacular night views, alongside Hakodate’s Mount Hakodate and Kobe’s Mount Rokko/Maya.

Access

The Nagasaki Ropeway runs from Fuchi Shrine station to the summit. The ride takes approximately 5 minutes and offers dramatic views throughout.

Tip

The ropeway operates until approximately 22:00. Visit after dark for the full “10 million dollar” night view experience. Clear evenings offer the most spectacular displays.

Visitor Tip

Nagasaki’s hilly terrain makes comfortable walking shoes essential. Start with the Peace Park and Museum in the morning, then explore Dejima and the historic center (Sofuku-ji, Megane Bridge, Dutch Slope) on foot. Visit Glover Garden in the afternoon for harbor views, and save Inasa-yama for after dark to experience one of Japan’s legendary night panoramas.
