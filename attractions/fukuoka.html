Gateway to Kyushu

Kyushu’s Gateway & Ramen Capital

Fukuoka is the dynamic, fast-growing gateway to Japan’s southern island of Kyushu — a city where ancient shrine traditions and cutting-edge contemporary architecture exist side by side. As one of the most livable cities in Asia, Fukuoka combines the energy of a major metropolis with a laid-back, welcoming atmosphere that draws visitors and new residents alike.

The city is legendary for its food culture, above all its yatai — open-air street food stalls that line the banks of the Naka River and the neon-lit streets of Nakasu every evening. With roughly 100 yatai still operating, Fukuoka is the last major city in Japan to preserve this intimate, communal dining tradition. Hakata tonkotsu ramen, with its rich, milky pork-bone broth and thin noodles, was born here and remains the city’s most iconic dish.

Beyond its culinary fame, Fukuoka offers remarkable cultural depth: the ancient Dazaifu Tenmangu shrine dedicated to the god of learning, the world’s largest reclining bronze Buddha at Nanzo-in Temple, and the expansive Ohori Park built around a former castle moat. It is a city that rewards those who look beyond the surface.

Nanzo-in Temple

南蔵院

Located in the quiet town of Sasaguri on the outskirts of Fukuoka, Nanzo-in Temple is home to the world’s largest reclining bronze Buddha — an awe-inspiring statue measuring 41 meters in length and weighing approximately 300 tons. Completed in 1995, this hidden gem remains virtually unknown to most international tourists.

World Record Hidden Gem
Significance

The reclining Buddha (Nehan-zo) depicts the moment of the Buddha’s entry into nirvana. Nanzo-in is also part of the 88-station Sasaguri pilgrimage, a miniature version of the famous Shikoku pilgrimage that winds through the surrounding forested hills.

Surroundings

The temple grounds are nestled within a university forest, providing a tranquil, deeply wooded setting. The contrast between the massive bronze Buddha and the quiet mountain landscape creates a profound sense of stillness.

Hours

Open daily 9:00–16:30. Easily reached by JR Sasaguri Line from Hakata Station (approximately 20 minutes). Free admission to temple grounds.

Ohori Park & Fukuoka Castle

大濠公園

A sprawling central park built around the former moat of Fukuoka Castle, Ohori Park features a 2-kilometer lake perimeter with three islands connected by elegant bridges. The adjacent Fukuoka Castle (Maizuru-jo) ruins represent the largest castle ever built in Kyushu, originally constructed in 1601.

Castle Ruins City Park
Castle

While the main keep no longer stands, the massive stone walls, restored gates, and elevated viewing platforms remain. From the upper terraces, visitors enjoy sweeping panoramic views of the city skyline and Hakata Bay.

Park

The park is one of Fukuoka’s most beloved green spaces, popular for jogging, boating, and evening strolls. In spring, over 1,000 cherry trees burst into bloom, making it one of the best hanami spots in Kyushu.

Access

Free and open 24 hours. Located in the heart of the city, easily reached from Ohori-koen Station on the Kuko subway line.

Dazaifu Tenmangu

太宰府天満宮

One of the most important Shinto shrines in all of Japan, Dazaifu Tenmangu is dedicated to Sugawara no Michizane, the deified patron of learning and scholarship. Legend holds that his beloved plum tree (tobiume) miraculously flew from Kyoto to Dazaifu to follow its master into exile. Students from across Japan make pilgrimages here before important exams.

Major Shrine God of Learning
Architecture

The new main hall, designed by acclaimed architect Sou Fujimoto, represents a striking fusion of contemporary design and sacred tradition. The approach to the shrine crosses arched bridges over ornamental ponds, lined with plum trees and traditional shops.

Culture

Try umegae mochi — grilled rice cakes filled with sweet red bean paste, a tradition stretching back over 1,000 years. The approach street also features a Starbucks designed by architect Kengo Kuma, constructed entirely from interlocking wooden beams without a single nail.

Hours

Open daily, generally 6:30–19:00 (seasonal variations). Free admission. Located approximately 30 minutes from Hakata Station by Nishitetsu train.

Fukuoka Food Culture

福岡グルメ

Fukuoka is the undisputed capital of Hakata tonkotsu ramen — a rich, creamy pork-bone broth served with thin, firm noodles and customizable toppings. The city’s approximately 100 remaining yatai (open-air food stalls) line the banks of the Naka River and the streets of Nakasu, offering an intimate, uniquely Fukuokan dining experience every evening.

Ramen Capital Yatai Culture
Specialties

Beyond ramen, Fukuoka is famous for mentaiko (spicy marinated cod roe), mizutaki (a delicate chicken hot pot in rich collagen broth), and motsu nabe (a hearty offal hot pot with garlic chives and cabbage). Each is a deeply local dish rarely found in the same quality elsewhere.

Yatai

The yatai experience is unique to Fukuoka — small, open-sided stalls seating 6–10 people, where strangers share counters and conversation over steaming bowls of ramen, grilled skewers, and cold beer. Most yatai open from early evening until around 2:00 AM.

Views

For panoramic views of the city and coastline, visit Fukuoka Tower — at 234 meters, it is the tallest seaside tower in Japan. The observation deck offers spectacular sunset and nighttime views over Hakata Bay.

Visitor Tip

Combine a morning trip to Dazaifu Tenmangu with an afternoon at Ohori Park and Fukuoka Castle ruins. In the evening, head to the Nakasu or Tenjin yatai for an authentic street-food dinner. For Nanzo-in Temple, go on a weekday morning for the most peaceful experience — you may have the giant Buddha nearly to yourself.
