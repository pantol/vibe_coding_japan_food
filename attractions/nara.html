Ancient Capital

Japan’s First Permanent Capital

Nara served as Japan’s first permanent capital from 710 to 784, a transformative era known as the Nara Period. During this time, Buddhism flourished under imperial patronage and Japan’s earliest written histories were compiled. The capital, known as Heijō-kyō, was modeled after the Tang Dynasty capital of Chang’an, reflecting the deep cultural exchange between Japan and continental Asia.

Today, over 1,200 sacred sika deer roam freely through the city’s parks and temple grounds, considered divine messengers in the Shinto tradition. The city’s great monuments — Todai-ji, Kasuga Taisha, and Kōfuku-ji — are designated UNESCO World Heritage Sites, preserving the spiritual and architectural legacy of Japan’s earliest imperial era.

Compact and remarkably walkable, Nara is an ideal day trip from Kyoto or Osaka, offering visitors a chance to step back over a thousand years into the origins of Japanese civilization, all while surrounded by gentle deer and ancient forests.

Nara Park (Nara-koen)

奈良公園

Over 1,200 sika deer roam freely through this vast park, considered divine messengers in the Shinto tradition. According to legend, the god Takemikazuchi arrived at Kasuga Taisha on a white deer in 768 AD. The deer are designated as Natural Monuments of Japan. The park is home to Nara’s greatest treasures: Todai-ji, Kasuga Taisha, and Kōfuku-ji.

Sacred Park Open 24/7 · Free
Todai-ji

Home to the Daibutsu — the world’s largest bronze Buddha statue, standing 15 meters tall and weighing approximately 500 tons. The Great Buddha was originally cast in 752 AD. The hall housing it, Daibutsuden, is one of the largest wooden buildings in the world. Hours: 7:30–17:30 (seasonal variations).

Kasuga Taisha

One of Japan’s most celebrated Shinto shrines, famous for its more than 3,000 stone and bronze lanterns donated by worshippers over the centuries. The lanterns are lit during the Mantoro festivals in February and August, creating an otherworldly atmosphere.

Kōfuku-ji

Founded in 669, this temple features a striking five-story pagoda — the second tallest in Japan. The temple was once one of the most powerful in the country, with its monks wielding significant political influence during the Nara and Heian periods.

History

Nara was Japan’s first permanent capital from 710 to 784 (Nara Period). Known as Heijō-kyō, the city was modeled after the Tang Dynasty capital of Chang’an. The monuments within the park are collectively designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Visitor Tip

Buy shika senbei (deer crackers, ~¥200) from vendors throughout the park to feed the deer — but be careful, they can be surprisingly pushy! Bow to the deer and they will often bow back. Nara Park is open 24 hours a day and free to enter. Todai-ji hours are 7:30–17:30 with seasonal variations. Leave large backpacks and bags in the coin lockers at Nara Station to avoid unwanted deer attention — they have been known to chew on bags, maps, and clothing.
